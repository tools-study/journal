 # Partial Reprogramming Review

The central obstacle to therapeutic partial reprogramming is the narrow margin between rejuvenation and dedifferentiation—a dosage-control problem compounded by oncogenic risk, tissue heterogeneity, and delivery constraints that together define the field’s critical path. Since Ocampo et al. demonstrated in 2016 that cyclic OSKM expression could ameliorate age-associated hallmarks in progeroid mice   (Cell, 2016; PMID: 27984723), the field has advanced rapidly: Lu et al. restored vision via OSK in aged mice   (Nature, 2020; PMID: 33268865),   Gill et al. rejuvenated human fibroblasts by ~30 epigenetic years (eLife, 2022; PMID: 35390271),  and in January 2026, Life Biosciences’ ER-100 became the first partial epigenetic reprogramming therapy to receive FDA IND clearance for human trials.  Yet fundamental challenges remain. This review ranks twelve interrelated challenges by priority for achieving safe, identity-preserving partial reprogramming of non-hematopoietic human tissues.

-----

## The priority hierarchy: three tiers of translational barriers

The twelve challenges fall into three priority tiers based on their position on the critical path to clinical translation. **Tier 1** comprises the three challenges that, if unsolved, preclude any safe therapeutic application: (1) dosage and duration control of reprogramming factors, (2) oncogenic risk and safety, and (3) epigenetic memory and fidelity. These are mechanistically entangled—inadequate dose control drives both oncogenic transformation and loss of cell identity. **Tier 2** encompasses challenges that determine whether a safe protocol can be deployed effectively: (4) in vivo delivery, (5) tissue-specific responses, (6) cellular heterogeneity, and (7) biomarkers and monitoring. **Tier 3** addresses challenges that govern long-term therapeutic value and population-scale applicability: (8) long-term stability, (9) senescence-SASP interactions, (10) species translation, (11) immune system interactions, and (12) scalability.

-----

## TIER 1, CHALLENGE 1: The dosage-duration control problem is the field’s rate-limiting constraint

The most fundamental challenge in partial reprogramming is calibrating factor expression to achieve rejuvenation while halting before cells cross an irreversible dedifferentiation threshold. This “Goldilocks problem” was first defined experimentally by Ocampo et al., who showed that continuous OSKM expression in transgenic mice caused weight loss, organ failure, and death within approximately four days,  while a **cyclic protocol of 2 days on and 5 days off**  extended median lifespan  by ~33% in LAKI progeroid mice   without teratoma formation   (Cell, 2016; PMID: 27984723). Critically, dose-dependency was strict: mice carrying a single copy of the OSKM transgene tolerated 35 cycles safely, but mice with **two transgene copies developed teratomas** in liver, kidney, and pancreas after only 8 cycles.  

Subsequent studies have refined our understanding of this window. Browder et al. applied the cyclic protocol to physiologically aged wild-type mice (starting at 15 months) for 7 months and achieved epigenetic clock reversal  of ~0.4 mouse years (~20 human-equivalent years)   in kidney and skin, with no adverse effects  on blood counts, neurological function, or gross histology  (Nature Aging, 2022; PMID: 37118377).  Importantly, a shorter 1-month treatment beginning at 25 months produced no detectable epigenetic clock improvement in any tissue,  suggesting a **cumulative threshold** for rejuvenation.   Gill et al. identified day 13 as the optimal “sweet spot” for their maturation phase transient reprogramming (MPTR) of human fibroblasts,   where epigenetic age reduction peaked at ~30 years;  paradoxically, days 15 and 17 showed *less* rejuvenation,   likely due to cellular stress from approaching the point of no return   (eLife, 2022; PMID: 35390271). 

The concept of a **point of no return** was computationally delineated by Olova et al., who analyzed a 49-day iPSC reprogramming time course and found that epigenetic age declines steadily from day 3 onward,  with a critical window between days 3–13 where rejuvenation occurs before somatic identity is irreversibly lost  (Aging Cell, 2019; PMID: 30450724).  Beyond approximately day 15 in human fibroblasts, cells enter the maturation phase of reprogramming and cannot revert.  Hishida et al. demonstrated that even **1 day** of hepatocyte-specific OSKM expression sufficed to  downregulate the master hepatocyte transcription factor Hnf4α, activate fetal genes (Afp), and remodel chromatin toward pluripotency-associated motifs—creating a “poised, plastic” state that enhanced regeneration following acetaminophen injury  (Cell Reports, 2022; PMID: 35476977).  This suggests that in highly responsive tissues, the margin between therapeutic effect and identity compromise may be extraordinarily narrow.

The OSK versus OSKM question represents a major strategic fork. Lu et al. demonstrated that **Oct4, Sox2, and Klf4 alone (without c-Myc)** are sufficient for epigenetic rejuvenation and functional restoration in retinal ganglion cells,   with no tumors observed after 15 months of continuous expression   (Nature, 2020; PMID: 33268865). Only the polycistronic construct was effective—monocistronic co-delivery failed due to inconsistent stoichiometry.  Macip et al. extended this to systemic delivery, showing AAV9-delivered inducible OSK in 124-week-old wild-type mice extended median remaining lifespan by **109%**  (Cellular Reprogramming, 2024; PMID: 38381405). The Alle et al. study further demonstrated that a single short reprogramming burst early in life could initiate a propagating epigenetic mechanism that extended healthy lifespan  by ~15%  in both LAKI and wild-type mice  (Aging Cell, 2022; PMID: 36207783).   Roux et al. at Calico screened all Yamanaka factor combinations via single-cell genomics  and found that **OS (Oct4+Sox2) alone** could restore youthful transcriptional profiles with minimal identity suppression,   while identifying MSX1 as a novel factor from amphibian regeneration biology (Cell Systems, 2022; PMID: 35690067). 

**Open critical questions.** No predictive biomarker exists to indicate when a cell approaches the point of no return in vivo. The relationship between factor expression level, duration, and tissue-specific dedifferentiation thresholds remains uncharacterized for most human cell types. Whether cyclic protocols are mechanistically superior to single extended pulses, or simply safer by limiting cumulative exposure, is unresolved.

## TIER 1, CHALLENGE 2: Oncogenic risk remains the primary barrier to regulatory approval

The oncogenic risk of reprogramming factor expression, particularly c-Myc, represents the single most critical safety concern for clinical translation. c-Myc is deregulated in up to **70% of human cancers**,  and its role in OSKM creates an inherent tension between reprogramming efficiency and transformation risk. Abad et al. provided the starkest demonstration: continuous in vivo OSKM expression in transgenic mice produced teratomas from stomach, intestine, pancreas, and kidney,  with circulating iPSCs in the blood that displayed totipotency features exceeding standard in vitro iPSCs  (Nature, 2013; PMID: 24025773).   Parras et al. subsequently showed that the primary cause of mortality from continuous OSKM is not teratoma formation but **hepatic and intestinal failure**,  as these tissues reprogram fastest due to high proliferative rates and doxycycline absorption kinetics  (Nature Aging, 2023; PMID: 38012287). 

The p53 tumor suppressor pathway creates a paradoxical dual role. Four landmark 2009 papers in Nature established that p53 deficiency dramatically increases reprogramming efficiency but simultaneously increases cancer risk:  Hong et al. (PMID: 19668191) showed p53-p21 pathway suppression of iPSC generation;  Kawamura et al. (PMID: 19668186) linked the p53 pathway to reprogramming barriers;  Marión et al. (PMID: 19668189) demonstrated p53-mediated DNA damage response limits on reprogramming to ensure genomic integrity;   and Li et al. (PMID: 19668188) identified the Ink4/Arf locus as a reprogramming barrier. Mosteiro et al. deepened this picture by showing that in vivo OSKM expression simultaneously induces reprogramming in some cells and **senescence in neighboring cells** through the Ink4a/Arf locus, with senescent cells secreting IL-6 that paradoxically *facilitates* reprogramming paracrinically  (Science, 2016; PMID: 27884981).  In p53-null mice, this cascade amplifies: more tissue damage leads to more senescence, more IL-6, more reprogramming,  and critically, more teratomas. 

Dropping c-Myc dramatically improves the safety profile.   Lu et al. reported zero tumors after 15 months of continuous OSK expression in the retina   (Nature, 2020; PMID: 33268865).  Sahu et al. from Altos Labs demonstrated that targeting OSK expression specifically to senescent and stressed cells  using the **Cdkn2a (p16) promoter** extended lifespan  by ~12% in normal mice and up to 40% in progeroid models without increased tumor occurrence   (Science Translational Medicine, 2024; PMID: 39259812).  This cell-state-specific targeting approach represents a significant safety advance, restricting reprogramming factor expression to cells that most need it while sparing healthy cells. 

The field must also contend with **cumulative mutational burden** in aged human tissues. Pre-existing somatic mutations in oncogenes or tumor suppressors—common in aged epithelia—could interact synergistically with reprogramming factor expression to promote transformation, a risk not modeled in young laboratory mice. No long-term safety data from non-transgenic delivery systems in aged animals exists beyond the Macip et al. study.

## TIER 1, CHALLENGE 3: Separating epigenetic age from epigenetic identity

The core theoretical challenge of partial reprogramming is whether aging-associated epigenetic marks can be selectively erased while preserving cell-type-specific epigenetic identity. The emerging evidence supports a model where these two information layers are mechanistically separable, but the separation is incomplete and context-dependent.

Yang et al. provided the most compelling mechanistic framework via the ICE (Inducible Changes to the Epigenome) mouse system, demonstrating that **epigenetic information loss is a cause—not merely a consequence—of mammalian aging**, and that cells retain a “backup copy” of youthful epigenetic instructions accessible via OSK (Cell, 2023; PMID: 36638792). In ICE mice subjected to 5 weeks of whole-body AAV-OSK, H3K9me3 was restored in kidneys and H3K36me2 in muscle to levels resembling young controls, with epigenetic age reversed by up to 57% across four independent mouse clocks.   This study established the **information theory of aging**: the epigenome degrades stochastically over time, but a referential template persists that reprogramming factors can read to restore youthful chromatin states. 

Gill et al. provided the strongest evidence for separability in human cells. During their 13-day MPTR protocol, **fibroblast-specific enhancers remained demethylated throughout** OSKM exposure,  serving as carriers of epigenetic identity memory that enabled cells to revert to a fibroblast state after factor withdrawal—but with a rejuvenated epigenome  (eLife, 2022; PMID: 35390271).  The Horvath multi-tissue clock and skin-and-blood clock both registered ~30 years of rejuvenation, while the transcriptome was rejuvenated by 20–40 years  and collagen production was restored to youthful levels.  Sarkar et al. achieved more modest rejuvenation (~1.5–4.9 years by Horvath clock) using only 4 days of mRNA-delivered OSKMLN in aged human fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and chondrocytes—  an “initiation phase” approach that preserved cell identity completely but provided less rejuvenation than Gill’s deeper “maturation phase” protocol (Nature Communications, 2020; PMID: 32210226).  

The distinction between **age-related CpG methylation drift** (occurring stochastically at clock CpG sites) and **cell-identity CpG methylation** (at lineage-specific enhancers and promoters) is now well-established but imperfectly characterized. Moqri et al. showed that ~90% of age-dependent DNA methylation gain occurs at **PRC2-bound low-methylated regions**, offering a mechanistically interpretable biomarker that appears universal across tissues and species   and is reduced by both caloric restriction and partial reprogramming (Nature Communications, 2024; PMID: 39009581).   This suggests that aging-associated methylation and identity-specifying methylation occupy largely distinct genomic compartments, which is encouraging for selective erasure. However, the boundaries are not absolute: Browder et al. found epigenetic clock reversal in kidney and skin but not in liver, lung, muscle, or spleen  despite similar transgene expression,  indicating that the ease of selectively resetting age marks varies dramatically across tissues  (Nature Aging, 2022; PMID: 37118377). 

The requirement for active DNA demethylation via **TET1 and TET2** enzymes was demonstrated by Lu et al., who showed that OSK-mediated vision restoration was abolished in TET1/TET2 double knockouts   (Nature, 2020; PMID: 33268865).  This positions TET-mediated hydroxymethylation as the molecular effector of epigenetic rejuvenation,  raising the question of whether TET activity can be directed toward age-associated marks without affecting identity marks. Reik and colleagues at the Babraham Institute have contributed foundational work on DNA methylation reprogramming dynamics, including the identification of tissue-specific enhancer methylation as a stable carrier of lineage information during reprogramming.

## TIER 2, CHALLENGE 4: Delivery to human tissues remains a formidable engineering problem

Translating partial reprogramming from transgenic mouse models to human therapy requires delivery systems that can express reprogramming factors at controlled levels in target tissues. Two platforms dominate: AAV gene therapy and mRNA-lipid nanoparticles (LNPs).

**AAV-based delivery** benefits from tissue tropism (AAV2 for retina, AAV8 for liver, AAV9 for heart/CNS/systemic), long-term episomal persistence enabling inducible activation, and clinical precedent.  Lu et al. used AAV2 with a Tet-Off system to achieve ~40% retinal ganglion cell transduction and sustained OSK expression for over 15 months without safety concerns  (Nature, 2020; PMID: 33268865). Only the polycistronic OSK construct was effective—co-delivery via separate monocistronic AAVs failed due to low co-infection frequency.  The ~4.7 kb AAV packaging limit  accommodates OSK as a polycistron but is too small for OSKM  (~5.7 kb combined coding sequence),  providing an additional practical argument for dropping c-Myc.  For systemic delivery, Macip et al. used dual AAV9 vectors (rtTA + TRE-OSK) intravenously in aged mice,  achieving high expression in liver and heart but not brain,  likely due to poor dual-vector co-transduction and limited doxycycline penetration across the blood-brain barrier  (Cellular Reprogramming, 2024; PMID: 38381405). 

**mRNA-LNP delivery** offers inherent safety advantages: transient expression (hours to days), no genomic integration, and dose-controllable pharmacokinetics. Sarkar et al. established proof-of-concept using modified mRNA cocktails (OSKMLN)  delivered via commercial transfection reagents  (Nature Communications, 2020; PMID: 32210226).  Turn Biotechnologies has developed the ERA (Epigenetic Reprogramming of Aging) platform  using proprietary eTurna LNPs, achieving approximately 30% transfection efficiency in skin cells  and securing a $300M licensing agreement with HanAll Biopharma for ophthalmologic applications.   A 2026 study demonstrated **hepatocyte-specific partial reprogramming** via a novel ionizable lipid (H4T3) enabling phospholipid-free LNP formulation with enhanced hepatocyte selectivity that attenuated liver fibrosis in mice  (Journal of Controlled Release, 2026). However, most LNPs preferentially accumulate in liver, and extrahepatic targeting—to lung, brain, muscle, or cartilage—remains a major unsolved problem.

**Key delivery constraints** include: (i) anti-capsid neutralizing antibodies present in 30–60% of humans  for AAV2,  preventing redosing; (ii) dose-dependent AAV toxicity including complement activation, dorsal root ganglia toxicity, and hepatotoxicity observed in high-dose clinical trials;   (iii) transgene “leakiness” from inducible promoters creating baseline expression; and (iv) manufacturing scalability, with AAV gene therapies currently costing  $1–3 million per patient. Life Biosciences’ ER-100 uses a doxycycline-inducible AAV-OSK system delivered via intravitreal injection—  the first partial reprogramming therapy to receive FDA IND clearance (January 2026, NCT07290244).

## TIER 2, CHALLENGE 5: Tissues reprogram at vastly different rates, creating a systemic safety problem

One of the most consequential findings in the field is that different tissues respond to reprogramming with markedly different kinetics, efficiency, and vulnerability. This tissue heterogeneity creates a fundamental dilemma for whole-body approaches: any systemic OSKM dose sufficient to reprogram resistant tissues will over-reprogram sensitive ones.

Parras et al. provided the most dramatic illustration, showing that continuous OSKM expression causes lethal hepatic and intestinal failure  within approximately one week because these tissues reprogram much faster than others—  likely due to high proliferative rates and doxycycline absorption dynamics  (Nature Aging, 2023; PMID: 38012287).  Liver and intestine showed the highest OSKM transcript levels of all organs.  When Parras et al. generated a novel transgenic strain that avoids OSKM expression in liver and intestine, toxicity was dramatically reduced and long-term continuous reprogramming became feasible.  Chondronasiou et al. found that the **pancreas was the most responsive tissue** to a single 1-week OSKM pulse in naturally aged mice, with more prominent rejuvenation of DNA methylation and transcriptomics than liver or spleen  (Aging Cell, 2022; PMID: 35235716).  Conversely, post-mitotic tissues like brain and skeletal muscle appear more resistant to reprogramming.

Tissue-specific studies have demonstrated rejuvenation across multiple organs individually. Chen et al. showed cardiomyocyte-specific short-term OSKM improved heart regeneration following myocardial infarction   by inducing adult cardiomyocytes to re-enter a fetal-like proliferative state—  but extended expression led to tumors  (Science, 2021; PMID: 34554778).  Wang et al. demonstrated that in vivo partial reprogramming enhances muscle regeneration by remodeling the stem cell niche  (Nature Communications, 2021; PMID: 34035273).  Xu et al. from the Brunet lab at Stanford showed that partial reprogramming restored **neuroblast proportions to youthful levels** in aged mouse brains, improving new neuron production in the neurogenic niche   (Nature Aging, 2024; PMID: 38553564).  Doeser et al. demonstrated that OSKM activation in cutaneous wounds reduced fibrosis and scar formation without teratoma formation  (Stem Cells, 2018; PMID: 29761584).  These results, while encouraging, underscore that optimal protocols must be tissue-specific—a single systemic regimen cannot serve all organs.

The landmark 2025 Altos Labs paper by Lu et al. introduced a unifying concept: **mesenchymal drift (MD)**—a pervasive, age-associated upregulation of mesenchymal gene programs observed across >40 human tissues and 20 diseases—that correlates with disease progression and mortality   and is reversed by partial reprogramming before dedifferentiation occurs  (Cell, 2025; PMID: 40816266). This finding suggests a shared downstream effector of tissue aging that partial reprogramming can target across diverse tissue types.

## TIER 2, CHALLENGE 6: Stochastic reprogramming creates a uniformity problem within tissues

Even within a single tissue, cells do not reprogram uniformly. Cellular heterogeneity in reprogramming response—driven by differences in epigenetic state, cell-cycle phase, transcription factor activity, and local microenvironment—means that any population-level treatment produces a distribution of outcomes ranging from no effect to over-reprogramming.

Buganim et al. identified a “stochastic phase” of reprogramming where gene activation/inactivation proceeds through probabilistic intermediates,  with bimodal distributions of gene expression before cells resolve into a fully reprogrammed state  (Cell, 2012; PMID: 22980981).  Francesconi et al. showed that **pre-existing Myc activity levels** in starting B cell populations predict reprogramming efficiency: Myc-high cells reprogram efficiently while Myc-low cells fail  (eLife, 2019; PMID: 30860479).   This suggests that cellular heterogeneity in reprogramming response is partly predetermined by the starting epigenetic and transcriptional state.

Roux et al. at Calico used single-cell genomics to map identity trajectories during partial reprogramming in murine adipogenic cells and mesenchymal stem cells, yielding several key insights: cells progressed along heterogeneous trajectories but tended to return to baseline states;  **no single Yamanaka factor was necessary** to restore youthful expression; and critically, the effects on aging gene expression and somatic identity suppression were **not tightly entangled**, suggesting it may be possible to rejuvenate without fully suppressing identity   (Cell Systems, 2022; PMID: 35690067). A 2025 Nature Genetics study using scTF-seq (single-cell transcription factor sequencing) revealed that TF dose shapes reprogramming heterogeneity through both dose-dependent and stochastic cell state transitions, with combinatorial TF interactions shifting from synergistic to antagonistic depending on relative dose.  This finding implies that achieving uniform partial reprogramming may require precisely matched factor stoichiometry—a challenge for viral and non-viral delivery alike.

The **fraction of cells that actually undergo meaningful partial reprogramming during in vivo treatments** remains largely unknown.  This is a critical gap: if only 5–10% of cells in a tissue reprogram, organismal rejuvenation may depend heavily on paracrine effects from reprogrammed cells rather than direct cell-autonomous rejuvenation.

## TIER 2, CHALLENGE 7: We lack real-time biomarkers to guide reprogramming depth

Measuring the degree of partial reprogramming—and determining when to stop—is essential for both research rigor and clinical safety. DNA methylation-based epigenetic clocks remain the gold standard. **Horvath’s multi-tissue clock**   uses 353 CpGs to estimate chronological age across 51 tissue/cell types   (Genome Biology, 2013; PMID: 24138928),  while **Hannum’s blood clock** uses 71 CpGs  (Molecular Cell, 2013; PMID: 23177740). Second-generation clocks include **PhenoAge** (Levine et al., Aging, 2018; PMID: 29676998), which incorporates clinical biomarkers to predict healthspan;  **GrimAge** (Lu et al., Aging, 2019; PMID: 30669119), the strongest mortality predictor using epigenetic proxies for plasma proteins;   and **DunedinPACE** (Belsky et al., eLife, 2022; PMID: 35029144), which measures the pace of aging rather than biological age per se.  

The Gladyshev laboratory has been central to advancing clock methodology. Ying et al. developed **DamAge and AdaptAge**—causality-enriched clocks that separate detrimental from adaptive methylation changes  via epigenome-wide Mendelian randomization  (Nature Aging, 2024; PMID: 38243142).  Tarkhov et al. delineated stochastic and co-regulated components of epigenetic aging at single-cell resolution  (Nature Aging, 2024; PMID: 38724733). Moqri et al. proposed the **PRC2-AgeIndex**, showing that ~90% of age-dependent methylation gain occurs at PRC2-bound low-methylated regions—a mechanistically interpretable biomarker applicable across tissues and species   (Nature Communications, 2024; PMID: 39009581). Trapp et al. developed single-cell epigenetic age profiling, enabling resolution of age heterogeneity within tissues (Nature Aging, 2021).

A critical methodological concern was raised by Kriukov et al., who showed that DNA methylation profiles during reprogramming are **poorly represented in aging training data**, introducing high epistemic uncertainty in epigenetic age estimations during reprogramming  (Aging Cell, 2024).  This means that when clocks report a “30-year rejuvenation” during MPTR, the estimate is extrapolated far outside the clocks’ training distribution—a sobering caveat for the field. Gill et al. used multiple independent clock algorithms and found that different clocks registered rejuvenation at different stages of reprogramming,   suggesting **staged epigenetic rejuvenation** rather than a single coordinated reset   (eLife, 2022; PMID: 35390271). 

**No real-time biomarker exists** for monitoring reprogramming depth in vivo. Current assays require tissue biopsy and DNA extraction. Fluorescent reporters of pluripotency markers, blood-based methylation proxies, and circulating epigenetic signatures are all areas of active development but remain unvalidated for clinical use.

## TIER 3, CHALLENGE 8: Rejuvenation effects may be transient without maintenance therapy

Whether partial reprogramming produces durable rejuvenation or requires chronic maintenance remains unresolved. Gill et al. found that human fibroblasts maintained a rejuvenated phenotype for at least **4–5 weeks** after OSKM withdrawal,  retaining lower epigenetic age, younger transcriptional profiles, and restored collagen production   (eLife, 2022; PMID: 35390271).   No study has tracked stability beyond this window in vitro. The Ocampo et al. chronic cyclic protocol (35 cycles in progeroid mice) strongly suggests that continuous treatment is necessary for sustained benefit  (Cell, 2016; PMID: 27984723).  Browder et al. reinforced this by showing that 7 months of cyclic OSKM was substantially more effective than 1 month  (Nature Aging, 2022; PMID: 37118377). 

Chondronasiou et al. provided some evidence for persistence: a single cycle of transient OSKM (1 week on, 2–4 weeks recovery) reversed DNA methylation changes in pancreas,  liver, spleen, and blood, with effects observed during the recovery period  (Aging Cell, 2022; PMID: 35235716).  However, the follow-up was limited. The fundamental question is whether the **aged tissue microenvironment**—including senescent neighbors, altered ECM, and systemic inflammatory signals—will progressively re-age rejuvenated cells, necessitating repeated treatments. This has profound implications for therapeutic design: a one-time gene therapy (AAV) versus chronic mRNA-LNP dosing represent fundamentally different paradigms depending on the answer.

## TIER 3, CHALLENGE 9: Senescence and reprogramming are paradoxically interdependent

The relationship between partial reprogramming and cellular senescence is bidirectional and creates a therapeutic paradox. Mosteiro et al. established that OSKM expression in vivo induces senescence (SA-β-gal+, p21+ cells) in some cells while promoting reprogramming in neighbors, with senescent cells providing essential IL-6 signaling that facilitates reprogramming  (Science, 2016; PMID: 27884981).   Anti-IL-6 antibodies reduced reprogramming by ~50%.  The senolytic navitoclax (ABT-263) cleared senescent cells but paradoxically reduced reprogramming efficiency, demonstrating that senescence is functionally required for efficient in vivo reprogramming.   Mosteiro et al. further dissected this mechanism, showing that **p16INK4a** specifically—not Arf—mediates the senescence-reprogramming link  (Aging Cell, 2018; PMID: 29280266).  Chiche et al. confirmed that injury-induced senescence enables reprogramming in skeletal muscle   (Cell Stem Cell, 2017; PMID: 28017795).  

On the therapeutic side, Browder et al. showed decreased senescence markers (p16, p21, p53, SA-β-gal) after long-term cyclic OSKM  (Nature Aging, 2022; PMID: 37118377).  A 2026 preprint by Núñez-Quintela et al. directly demonstrated that OSKM expression—or a reduced two-compound chemical regimen (tranylcypromine + RepSox)—attenuates SASP, restores mitochondrial homeostasis, and improves apoptotic priming in aged mice, establishing partial reprogramming as a **“senomorphic” intervention** that modulates rather than eliminates senescent cells.  This raises a strategic question: should senolytics and partial reprogramming be used sequentially (senolytics first to clear dysfunctional senescent cells, then reprogramming to rejuvenate surviving cells) or would this combination be counterproductive?

## TIER 3, CHALLENGE 10: The mouse-human gap is wider than the field acknowledges

Translation from mouse to human faces fundamental biological barriers beyond dose scaling. Human iPSC reprogramming takes **~21–30 days** versus ~10–14  days in mouse,  and the two species target different pluripotent states:  mouse iPSCs reach the naïve state (LIF-dependent) while human iPSCs reach the primed state (FGF/TGFβ-dependent)  (Teshigawara et al., Laboratory Investigation, 2017; PMID: 28792005). Chronis et al. found that while OSKM bind similar motifs in both species, actual binding events at specific syntenic loci show limited conservation, with human OSK factors targeting more closed chromatin sites  (Cell, 2017; PMID: 28111071). The foundational Ocampo et al. study used LAKI progeroid mice carrying a Lmna mutation causing Hutchinson-Gilford progeria syndrome—a specific nuclear envelope pathology that OSKM may particularly address—raising caution about extrapolating those dramatic results to normal human aging.

Human cell studies provide more directly translatable evidence. Gill et al. (eLife, 2022; PMID: 35390271) and Sarkar et al. (Nature Communications, 2020; PMID: 32210226) confirmed that partial reprogramming works in primary human cells from aged donors. However, the in vitro setting lacks the complexity of the in vivo tissue microenvironment. Life Biosciences’ Phase 1 trial (NCT07290244)  and YouthBio Therapeutics’ FDA INTERACT meeting for brain-targeted OSKM in Alzheimer’s disease (September 2025) represent the first steps toward human in vivo data. Species-comparative epigenetic clock calibration remains approximate: Browder et al.’s 0.4-year clock reversal in mice was estimated as “equivalent to 20 human years,” but this scaling lacks rigorous validation (Puri & Wagner, BioEssays, 2023; PMID: 36871150). The recent validation of partial reprogramming in aged rats by Horvath et al., showing improved cognitive function following hippocampal OSKM gene therapy (GeroScience, 2025), provides a first step toward larger-animal validation.

## TIER 3, CHALLENGE 11: Immune responses threaten delivery but remain understudied for reprogramming factors

The immunological challenges of partial reprogramming operate at two levels: vector immunogenicity and potential immunogenicity of the reprogramming factors themselves. AAV capsid immunogenicity is well-characterized: **30–60% of humans carry neutralizing antibodies** against AAV2, and first-dose administration generates robust humoral and cellular immune responses that preclude redosing—a critical barrier for cyclic reprogramming protocols. Capsid-specific CD8+ T cells can eliminate transduced cells weeks after gene transfer. Dose-dependent toxicity including complement activation, hepatotoxicity, and dorsal root ganglia toxicity has been observed in high-dose gene therapy trials (Verdera et al., Molecular Therapy, 2020; Mingozzi & High, Blood, 2013).

Whether OSKM factors themselves are immunogenic when transiently expressed in adult tissues is **essentially unstudied**. Oct4 and SOX2 are normally silenced in adult somatic cells but re-expressed in certain cancers, where they have been investigated as cancer vaccine targets—suggesting they can break tolerance in certain contexts. Whether partially reprogrammed cells express neoantigens or downregulate MHC class I molecules (as embryonic stem cells do), making them targets for NK cell-mediated killing, is entirely unknown. mRNA-LNP delivery avoids DNA integration but can trigger innate immune activation through lipid-mediated complement activation. Tissue-specific immune privilege varies: the eye (Lu et al.’s target) has relative immune privilege, while systemically accessible tissues do not. This knowledge gap represents a significant risk for clinical translation that pre-clinical programs must address.

## TIER 3, CHALLENGE 12: Scaling to whole-body rejuvenation requires solving delivery, heterogeneity, and cost simultaneously

Achieving organismal-level rejuvenation requires delivering reprogramming factors to a sufficient fraction of cells across the entire body. The fraction needed for tissue-level benefit is unknown but may be lower than expected due to paracrine amplification: Mosteiro et al. showed that SASP from senescent cells amplifies reprogramming in neighbors (Science, 2016; PMID: 27884981), and rejuvenated cells may similarly benefit their microenvironment. The two main scalable delivery platforms—AAV and mRNA-LNP—face complementary limitations. AAV provides durable expression but cannot be redosed due to immune memory and costs $1–3 million per patient at current manufacturing scales. mRNA-LNP is scalable (billions of COVID vaccine doses were manufactured) and inherently transient but requires repeated administration and is primarily liver-tropic with current formulations.

Retro Biosciences, in collaboration with OpenAI, used GPT-4b micro to redesign Yamanaka factors, creating **“RetroSOX” and “RetroKLF”** variants showing >50-fold increases in pluripotency marker expression and reducing iPSC generation from 3 weeks to 7 days (announced August 2025). While this was designed for iPSC generation rather than partial reprogramming, AI-optimized reprogramming factors could dramatically reduce the expression levels needed for rejuvenation, easing delivery constraints. NewLimit has tested **>1000× more reprogramming payloads** than the rest of the field combined using their high-throughput screening platform and identified novel transcription factor sets distinct from Yamanaka factors that reduce cell age while maintaining identity—potentially circumventing the oncogenic risks of OSKM entirely. Chemical reprogramming offers the ultimate scalability solution: Yang et al. from the Sinclair lab identified six chemical cocktails that restore youthful transcriptomic profiles in human cells within one week without genetic modification (Aging, 2023; PMID: 37437248), though the Gladyshev lab has cautioned that in vivo chemical reprogramming is associated with **toxic accumulation of lipid droplets** hindering rejuvenation (Aging Cell, 2026).

## The industry landscape has reached a clinical inflection point

The partial reprogramming field has attracted over **$8 billion in aggregate investment** and reached a historic milestone: Life Biosciences’ ER-100 became the first partial epigenetic reprogramming therapy to receive FDA IND clearance on January 28, 2026, initiating a Phase 1 trial (NCT07290244) for open-angle glaucoma and non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy using intravitreal AAV-OSK with doxycycline induction. Key pipeline developments include:

- **Altos Labs** (~$5.5B raised): Appointed CMO Joan Mannick in August 2025, signaling clinical readiness. Published landmark Cell paper on mesenchymal drift (Lu et al., 2025; PMID: 40816266) and Science Translational Medicine paper on targeted partial reprogramming (Sahu et al., 2024; PMID: 39259812). ## Conclusion: the field needs precision before scale

The twelve challenges outlined here converge on a single meta-problem: **partial reprogramming requires unprecedented precision of biological control**—over factor dosage, duration, tissue targeting, and cellular outcome—that current tools do not yet provide. The field’s critical path runs through three breakthrough requirements. First, development of real-time in vivo biomarkers that can guide reprogramming depth, enabling closed-loop control analogous to glucose monitoring in diabetes. Current epigenetic clocks, while powerful retrospectively, cannot serve this function. Second, identification of the molecular determinants that separate age-associated epigenetic marks from identity-specifying marks at single-cell resolution, enabling rational design of factors or small molecules that target one without disrupting the other. The PRC2-AgeIndex framework and TET-mediated demethylation pathway offer promising mechanistic handles. Third, delivery systems capable of tissue-specific, dose-controllable, repeatable administration—likely requiring either next-generation targeted LNPs or engineered AAV capsids with immunoevasive properties.

The trajectory is clear: from Takahashi and Yamanaka’s original demonstration that four transcription factors can reset cellular identity (Cell, 2006; PMID: 16904174), through Ocampo’s proof that partial reset can rejuvenate without dedifferentiating (Cell, 2016; PMID: 27984723), to Life Biosciences’ first human trial in 2026, the field has moved from impossible to implausible to investigational in two decades. The challenges are formidable but increasingly well-characterized. What was once a binary switch—differentiated or pluripotent—is revealing itself as a tunable dial. Learning to turn that dial with precision is the defining challenge of the next decade of aging biology.
