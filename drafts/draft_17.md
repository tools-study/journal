# Synthetic Biology Review

Synthetic biology has crossed a decisive threshold from laboratory curiosity to clinical reality. Recently, the field produced an unprecedented volume of translational breakthroughs: the first personalized CRISPR base-editing therapy administered to a living infant, FDA-approved clinical trials for pig-to-human organ transplantation, logic-gated CAR-T cells entering Phase 1 trials in solid tumors, AI-designed genome editors outperforming natural CRISPR-Cas9, and engineered living therapeutics advancing to Phase 3. These developments collectively signal that the design-build-test cycle of biological engineering has matured sufficiently to reshape medicine. This review synthesizes recent advances across fifteen domains where synthetic biology intersects human health, evaluating the most promising approaches, their translational trajectories, and remaining barriers to clinical deployment.

-----

## 1. AI-Designed Proteins and Genome Editors Are Rewriting the Rules of Drug Development

AlphaFold ratified a paradigm shift already underway: computational and AI-driven protein design has become a primary engine of therapeutic innovation.

RFdiffusion3, the latest foundation model from Baker’s Institute for Protein Design, consolidates multiple prior tools into a unified atom-level generative framework, designing proteins that interact with DNA, small molecules, and other proteins at 10× the speed of its predecessor [1]. Its precursor, RFdiffusion2, solved all 41 active sites in the AME enzyme benchmark — more than double the 16 achieved by any prior method — and generated enzymes with catalytic proficiencies approaching natural enzymes [2]. In a landmark demonstration, the Baker lab computationally designed serine hydrolases achieving catalytic efficiencies up to 2.2 × 10⁵ M⁻¹ s⁻¹, representing a 60,000-fold improvement over the closest comparable de novo enzyme design and featuring five entirely novel protein folds [3].

The practical impact is already entering the clinic. BindCraft, an open-source tool leveraging AlphaFold2 weights via backpropagation, achieves 10–100% experimental success rates (average 46.3%) for generating nanomolar-affinity protein binders without high-throughput screening [4]. Absci Corporation’s ABS-101, an AI-designed anti-TL1A antibody for inflammatory bowel disease, entered Phase 1 trials in May 2025 — reaching clinical stage in approximately two years versus the industry average of 5.5 years [5]. De novo designed proteins have neutralized lethal snake venom toxins with 80–100% mouse survival [6], and the first atomically accurate de novo antibodies targeting influenza hemagglutinin and *C. difficile* toxin B were validated by cryo-EM [7].

Perhaps most transformative for synthetic biology’s toolkit, OpenCRISPR-1 — an AI-designed genome editor generated by Profluent Bio’s ProGen2 language model trained on 5.1 million Cas proteins from 26.2 terabases of microbial genomes — achieves comparable on-target editing activity to SpCas9 (55.7% vs. 48.3%) with a 95% reduction in off-target editing [8]. The system is 403 amino acid mutations from SpCas9, demonstrating that AI can design functional gene editors entirely beyond the reach of natural evolution. At the frontier of protein language models, EvolutionaryScale’s ESM3, a 98-billion-parameter multimodal generative model, created esmGFP — a functional fluorescent protein at only 58% sequence identity to the closest known GFP, equivalent to approximately 500 million years of natural evolution compressed into a single computational generation [9].

The convergence of AI with synthetic biology extends beyond protein design. CRISPR-GPT, developed at Stanford, enables novice researchers to successfully perform gene knockouts on their first attempt using LLM-guided experimental design [10]. Pythia, from the University of Zurich, uses deep learning to predict DNA repair outcomes and optimize CRISPR editing templates for precise genome integrations [11]. These tools are democratizing biological engineering, collapsing timelines and expertise barriers that previously gated therapeutic development.

## 2. Base Editing Achieves Clinical Proof-of-Concept Across Multiple Diseases

Gene editing has progressed from proof-of-concept to clinical deployment at remarkable speed, with base editing now demonstrating efficacy in at least five distinct therapeutic programs and prime editing generating its first human data.

The most compelling single result is the personalized N-of-1 base editing therapy reported in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in May 2025. An infant, KJ, with severe carbamoyl phosphate synthetase 1 (CPS1) deficiency received a custom-designed adenine base editor (“k-abe”) delivered via lipid nanoparticle, developed and administered within just six months from diagnosis. After three doses, KJ tolerated increased protein intake and reduced nitrogen-scavenger medication with no serious adverse events [12]. This case establishes that personalized genome medicine is technically feasible at clinically meaningful timescales.

Verve Therapeutics’ VERVE-102 targeting PCSK9 for familial hypercholesterolemia has produced the most striking in vivo base editing dose-response data to date. In the Heart-2 Phase 1b trial (NCT06164730), the 0.6 mg/kg dose cohort achieved mean LDL-C reductions of 53% (maximum 69%) with PCSK9 protein reductions of 60%, sustained durability data from VERVE-101 extending to approximately two years, and no treatment-related serious adverse events. The proprietary GalNAc-LNP delivery system targets both LDLR and ASGPR receptors on hepatocytes, enabling liver-specific editing. Eli Lilly has committed to a 33% development cost share, signaling major pharmaceutical industry validation [13].

Beam Therapeutics has become the most prolific clinical developer of base editing. BEAM-302 for alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD) delivered a landmark: the first clinical genetic correction of a disease-causing point mutation via in vivo base editing. At the 60 mg dose, patients achieved mean total AAT of 12.4 µM at Day 28 — exceeding the 11 µM protective threshold — with mutant Z-AAT reduction of up to 78% (Beam Therapeutics, March 2025). The FDA aligned on a potential accelerated approval pathway [14].

For sickle cell disease, BEAM-101 (risto-cel) has treated 30 patients; all 17 patients evaluated at data cutoff achieved endogenous HbF greater than 60% with HbS reduction below 40%, with pancellular HbF distribution. BLA submission is anticipated by year-end 2026 [15]. CorrectSequence Therapeutics’ CS-101, using Transformer Base Editing (tBE) technology, reported six-month data from the first SCD patient showing HbF-to-HbS ratios stabilized at 6.5:3.5, total hemoglobin consistently above 120 g/L (from baseline 67.3 g/L), and no vaso-occlusive crises [16]. As of October 2025, 23 base editing therapies are undergoing clinical trials globally, with over half yielding initial results showing consistent clinical activity [17].

## 3. Prime Editing and Epigenome Editing Expand the Therapeutic Repertoire

While base editing dominates current clinical pipelines, prime editing and epigenome editing are advancing rapidly toward their own clinical inflection points.

Prime editing generated its first human clinical data in May 2025, when Prime Medicine reported positive results from a Phase 1/2 trial correcting the NCF1 mutation in chronic granulomatous disease (CGD). The underlying technology continues to improve dramatically. David Liu’s laboratory introduced PERT (Prime Editing-mediated Readthrough of Premature Termination codons), a disease-agnostic strategy that converts endogenous tRNA into optimized suppressor tRNA at a single genomic locus, potentially addressing the approximately 24–30% of rare diseases caused by nonsense mutations. PERT restored protein production with no detected off-target edits in models of Batten disease, Tay-Sachs disease, and Niemann-Pick type C1, and rescued extensive disease pathology in a mouse model of Hurler syndrome [18]. An MIT team developed modified Cas9 variants reducing prime editing error rates from 1 in 7 to 1 in 101 for the most-used editing mode, and from 1 in 122 to 1 in 543 in high-precision mode [19]. The eePASSIGE system combines prime editing with evolved recombinases for entire gene-sized integrations at native genomic locations [20], while ProPE extends the PE editing distance using a second non-cleaving sgRNA, achieving a 6.2-fold increase in overall editing efficiency [21].

Beyond CRISPR, two entirely new genome engineering modalities emerged in 2025. evoCAST, a laboratory-evolved CRISPR-associated transposase, achieved the first therapeutically relevant gene integration in human cells — over 200-fold improved integration activity over natural CASTs, with 10–30% integration of kilobase-size DNA cargoes at 14 genomic targets including safe harbor loci, no detectable indels, and low off-target integration [22]. Bridge recombinases from the Arc Institute demonstrated megabase-scale genome rearrangements in human cells — insertions, excisions, and inversions up to 0.93 megabases with up to 20% insertion efficiency and 82% genome-wide specificity [23]. These tools move beyond CRISPR’s “find-and-cut” paradigm to enable “cut-and-paste” of entire genomic regions.

Epigenome editing represents perhaps the most conceptually elegant advance, offering gene silencing without DNA double-strand breaks and — critically — the possibility of reversibility. The CHARM (Coupled Histone tail for Autoinhibition Release of Methyltransferase) editor, delivered via engineered AAV crossing the blood-brain barrier, achieved greater than 80% brainwide reduction in neuronal prion protein, the first demonstration of AAV-mediated epigenetic editing with durable gene silencing in the brain [24]. A self-silencing CHARM variant autonomously deactivates after silencing the target, enhancing safety. For cardiovascular disease, an epigenetic editor targeting PCSK9 via DNA methylation achieved near-complete silencing durable for over one year in transgenic mice, maintained after liver regeneration — and reversibility was demonstrated using a targeted epigenetic activator that restored PCSK9 expression [25]. The EvoETR platform achieved approximately 70% PCSK9 reduction persisting for up to 330 days from a single LNP dose [26]. Multiple companies are entering clinical development: Epicrispr Biotechnologies with EPI-321 for facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy (FSHD), Omega Therapeutics with OTX-2002 for hepatocellular carcinoma targeting MYC, and Tune Therapeutics with Tune-401 for hepatitis B virus [27].

## 4. Logic-Gated CAR-T Cells and Engineered Immunity Enter the Clinic

The application of synthetic biology principles to immunotherapy has produced a new generation of engineered immune cells that implement computational logic, address solid tumors, and expand beyond traditional autologous manufacturing.

The SNIPR (Synthetic Intramembrane Proteolysis Receptor) platform, published in *Nature* in February 2025, represents a conceptual breakthrough: compact, single-chain receptors that enable engineered cells to detect soluble tumor-associated factors like TGF-β and VEGF via ligand-induced dimerization and endocytic pH-dependent cleavage. SNIPR-CAR T cells demonstrated improved efficacy in lung adenocarcinoma and melanoma xenograft models with reduced off-target toxicity versus constitutive CARs [28]. A complementary approach from the Crystal Mackall laboratory introduced fuzzy logic into CAR-T cells through an Antagonism-Enforced Braking System (AEBS), where strong TCR-neoantigen interactions enhance CAR activation while weak TCR-self-antigen interactions antagonize it — maintaining antitumor activity against lung adenocarcinoma while reducing on-target/off-tumor toxicity [29].

These synthetic biology approaches are now entering clinical evaluation. The A2B694 Tmod CAR-T (A2 Biotherapeutics) implementing a NOT gate — an activator CAR targeting mesothelin paired with a blocker CAR recognizing HLA-A*02 to protect normal tissues — reported initial Phase 1 data at ASCO 2025 (EVEREST-2 trial, NCT06051695): no dose-limiting toxicities, no cytokine release syndrome, no neurotoxicity, and confirmed CAR-T cell expansion with tumor infiltration detected in pancreatic tumor biopsy 42 days post-infusion [30]. SENTI-202, the first logic-gated CAR-NK cell therapy, implements an OR-gate targeting CD33 or FLT3 combined with an inhibitory receptor for EMCN on normal hematopoietic stem cells, and achieved complete remissions in relapsed/refractory AML patients in Phase 1 [31].

Allogeneic “off-the-shelf” CAR-T cells have reached a critical milestone. WU-CART-007 (soficabtagene geleucel), a CRISPR/Cas9-edited allogeneic anti-CD7 CAR-T for T-cell malignancies, achieved an overall response rate of 90.9% with a complete response rate of 72.7% in evaluable patients at the recommended Phase 2 dose, earning FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designation in January 2026 [32]. The first allogeneic CAR-T for glioblastoma (MT026, CRISPR-edited IL-13Rα2-targeting) demonstrated 1 complete and 3 partial responses among 5 patients with intrathecal delivery [33].

CAR-macrophage therapy achieved its first human validation. CT-0508, an anti-HER2 CAR-macrophage from Carisma Therapeutics, was well-tolerated in 14 patients with no dose-limiting toxicities and no requirement for lymphodepletion [34]. A novel anti-TNF CAR-macrophage for inflammatory diseases, where TNF recognition activates intracellular IL-4 signaling to reprogram macrophages toward an anti-inflammatory phenotype, showed efficacy in kidney injury models with the phenotype automatically switching off during recovery [35].

AI is now accelerating CAR-T cell design. A CRISPR screening platform called CELLFIE identified RHOG knockout as a potent and unexpected CAR-T cell enhancer, validated across multiple in vivo models and patient-derived cells [36]. The CARMSeD predictive model screened 1,452 CARs for self-activation and dysfunction, enabling optimized bispecific CD20/CD19 CARs that eradicated tumors even in CD19/CD20-negative malignancies through a secretable CD3/CD22 bispecific engager [37]. India’s first homegrown CAR-T product, talicabtagene autoleucel, has treated over 400 patients at approximately $30,000 per treatment — 6% of Western product costs — with a 99% manufacturing success rate, demonstrating that global access is achievable [38].

## 5. Engineered Living Therapeutics Mature from Concept to Pivotal Clinical Trials

The World Economic Forum named engineered living therapeutics one of the top ten emerging technologies of 2025, reflecting a field that has progressed from preclinical curiosity to clinical-stage programs across oncology, metabolic disease, and infectious disease.

The most advanced synthetic biology-derived living therapeutic is Synlogic’s SYNB1934 (labafenogene marselecobac) for phenylketonuria (PKU), currently in a pivotal global Phase 3 trial (Synpheny-3). The engineered *E. coli* Nissle 1917 expresses phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) and L-amino acid deaminase (LAAD) to degrade phenylalanine in the gut. Phase 2 data showed a 43% reduction in D5-Phe AUC and 40% reduction in fasting plasma phenylalanine with no serious adverse events [39]. A conceptually important milestone was achieved with the first demonstration of controlled, stable colonization of the human gut with an engineered microbe [40], solving a longstanding challenge for deploying engineered bacteria as sustained therapeutics.

Novel engineering approaches are expanding the functional repertoire of therapeutic bacteria. The BACTRINS platform, using CRISPR-associated transposases delivered by conjugation, achieved 100% cargo transfer efficiency against Shiga toxin-producing *E. coli* in the mouse gut, sharply reducing toxin levels and providing complete protection against subsequent infections [41]. A complementary approach used phage-derived particles to deliver base editors to gut bacteria, achieving 93% median editing efficiency of target bacterial populations with a single dose, with edited bacteria stably maintained for 42 or more days [42]. The MetaEdit platform demonstrated in vivo genetic capture of native murine *Bacteroides* using CRISPR-associated transposases delivered by broadly conjugative vectors, with integration efficiency exceeding 70% [43]. These tools collectively enable species-specific, precise modification of the gut microbiome without requiring engineered organisms to persist indefinitely.

For cancer, engineered bacteria are being designed with increasing sophistication. *Clostridium sporogenes* engineered with a heterologous quorum sensing circuit infiltrates oxygen-starved tumor cores, activating an oxygen-tolerance gene only when sufficient bacterial numbers accumulate to prevent premature activation in the bloodstream [44]. High-tryptophan-expressing *C. butyricum* inhibits the IDO pathway via butyrate production, restoring CD8+ T cell function and significantly delaying tumor growth in preclinical models [45]. Engineered bacteria expressing a non-pathogenic IL-18 mutein boost both anti-tumor and CAR-NK cell responses, demonstrating synergy between engineered bacteria and cellular immunotherapy [46].

The clinical pipeline extends beyond oncology. Vedanta Biosciences’ VE303, an orally administered defined bacterial consortium of 8 clonal *Clostridia* strains for recurrent *C. difficile* infection, is in Phase 3 (RESTORATiVE303) with top-line results anticipated in 2026 [47]. Seres Therapeutics’ VOWST (SER-109), the first FDA-approved orally administered microbiota-based therapeutic (April 2023), established the commercial viability of the microbiome therapeutic paradigm with 88% recurrence-free rates at 8 weeks in Phase 3 [48]. MaaT Pharma’s MaaT013 reported Phase 3 success for acute graft-versus-host disease in 2025, potentially becoming the first approved microbiome drug in Europe [49].

## 6. mRNA Engineering and Circular RNA Open New Therapeutic Modalities

The mRNA revolution triggered by COVID-19 vaccines is expanding into non-vaccine therapeutics with remarkable breadth, aided by AI-driven sequence design and novel RNA formats.

The most transformative development in mRNA design is GEMORNA (Generative Models for RNA), published in *Science* in 2025, which uses transformer architectures to design complete mRNA sequences — both coding sequences and untranslated regions — from scratch. GEMORNA-designed mRNAs achieved up to 41-fold increases in protein expression in vitro and 15-fold enhancement in human EPO expression in mice, substantially outperforming codon-optimized benchmarks [50]. Multiple complementary AI tools have emerged: mRNABERT as a foundational model for complete mRNA sequence design [51] and RiboDecode for optimized codon sequences [52]. A tumor-selective mRNA system, cSMRTS, uses cancer-related miRNAs to control a Cas6-based on/off switch, achieving tumor suppression in two mouse cancer models through inherent tumor selectivity without external targeting [53].

Circular RNA (circRNA) has emerged as a clinically viable format. RiboX Therapeutics received the first-ever FDA IND clearance for a circular RNA therapy (RXRG001) in 2025, encoding human aquaporin 1 for radiation-induced xerostomia [54]. CircRNA’s covalently closed-loop structure confers enhanced stability, with sustained expression for up to 168 hours versus less than 20 hours for linear mRNA, and cap-independent translation via IRES elements [55]. CirCode Biotherapeutics’ HM2002 became the first circRNA biologic approved by China’s NMPA for ischemic heart disease [56]. Manufacturing costs remain approximately 10-fold higher than linear mRNA, but for applications requiring sustained local protein depots or durable antigen expression, circRNA offers clear advantages [57].

Self-amplifying RNA (saRNA) achieved full regulatory approval in major markets, with ARCT-154 receiving EU marketing authorization in 2025 following earlier Japanese approval. saRNA requires 10–100-fold lower doses than conventional mRNA while maintaining therapeutic protein levels for up to one month [58]. Beyond vaccines, MTL-CEBPA, a saRNA therapeutic for hepatocellular carcinoma, is in Phase 1 trials [59].

Moderna’s non-vaccine mRNA pipeline demonstrates the breadth of therapeutic opportunity. mRNA-3927 for propionic acidemia represents the first published clinical data for intracellular protein replacement via mRNA, with early signs of clinical benefit and over 15 person-years of treatment administered [60]. mRNA-4157/V940, a personalized cancer vaccine, achieved a 44% reduction in melanoma recurrence risk (HR=0.56) versus pembrolizumab monotherapy at three-year follow-up, with Phase 3 expansion and regulatory submissions anticipated in 2026 [61]. The pharmacokinetic profile of mRNA therapeutics is becoming better characterized: single mRNA molecules produce 10³–10⁶ protein copies, with expression onset at 2–6 hours, peak at 24–48 hours, decline over 7–14 days, and 5–50-fold variability in protein expression between patients — a challenge that precision dosing strategies must address [62].

## 7. Cell-Free Diagnostics and Programmable Biosensors Approach Clinical Deployment

Cell-free synthetic biology has matured into a robust diagnostic platform, offering room-temperature stability, sub-dollar costs, and attomolar sensitivity for point-of-care applications.

Cell-free protein synthesis (CFPS) systems now achieve yields up to 2.3 g/L for model proteins, with lyophilized systems stable at room temperature for weeks and costs reduced to approximately $0.04 per sensor for in-house preparations [63]. A cell-free immuno-profiling biochip using photolithographically patterned nanoliter compartments profiles over 30 SARS-CoV-2 viral epitopes simultaneously from just 1 µL of human serum, enabling quantitative antibody affinity determination without protein purification [64]. Toehold switch-based biosensors combined with NASBA amplification achieve limits of detection as low as 2.9 attomolar for Zika virus detection — comparable to RT-qPCR at a fraction of the cost [65].

CRISPR-based diagnostics continue to advance technically, though regulatory approval remains elusive. The ADESSO platform achieves a limit of detection of 2.5 copies/µL for SARS-CoV-2 with multiplexed variant detection [66], while Sherlock Biosciences (acquired by OraSure Technologies in 2024) and Mammoth Biosciences are pursuing commercialization pathways [67]. As of 2025, no CRISPR diagnostic assay has received full FDA approval for routine clinical use; previous Emergency Use Authorizations for COVID tests were temporary [68]. The push toward multiplexed, one-pot assays integrated with lateral flow formats represents the most promising path to market [69].

The convergence of synthetic biology with bioelectronics is creating novel diagnostic modalities. The IMBED (Ingestible Micro-Bio-Electronic Device) from Timothy Lu’s laboratory at MIT packages engineered probiotic biosensor bacteria with electronic readout in a swallowable capsule for real-time gastrointestinal health monitoring [70]. A *Bacteroides* synthetic biology toolkit enables non-invasive gut osmolality monitoring that can detect malabsorption severity from fecal samples even before clinical symptoms appear [71]. Wearable biosensors embedding freeze-dried cell-free synthetic circuits in textiles detect pathogen nucleic acid signatures at sensitivities rivaling qPCR [72]. These integrated platforms signal a future where synthetic biology enables continuous, non-invasive health monitoring.

## 8. Xenotransplantation Achieves Clinical Viability with Gene-Edited Pig Organs

Xenotransplantation underwent a transformation from experimental concept to regulated clinical procedure in 2025, with the FDA approving the first formal clinical trials and multiple organ types successfully transplanted into human recipients.

The landmark clinical report came from Massachusetts General Hospital, where Richard Slayman received an eGenesis pig kidney with 69 genomic edits — deletion of 3 glycan antigens (GGTA1, β4GalNT2, CMAH), insertion of 7 human transgenes (CD46, CD55, EPCR, TBM, CD47, HO1, A20), and inactivation of all porcine endogenous retroviruses [73]. The xenograft provided immediate function, creatinine levels decreased promptly, and dialysis was no longer needed. The patient survived 52 days before death from unrelated cardiac causes; autopsy showed no evident xenograft rejection [74]. A second MGH patient, Tim Andrews, achieved a record survival of 271 days with an eGenesis kidney before removal due to proteinuria; he remains alive on dialysis [75]. A third patient, Bill Stewart, returned home and resumed work after xenotransplantation [76].

Multi-omics analysis has revealed the immunological landscape in unprecedented detail. Despite profound T-cell depletion, early T-cell–mediated rejection occurred within one week in Slayman’s case, likely driven by subtherapeutic immunosuppression and residual CD8+ T cells in lymph nodes. Sustained innate immune activation — elevated monocyte/macrophage activity, IL-1β, and G-CSF — persisted even after adaptive immunity was controlled, establishing innate immunity as a major ongoing barrier [77]. NYU Langone’s 61-day brain-dead decedent study demonstrated that pig kidneys can effectively replace human kidney function, achieving hemodynamic stability and dialysis independence, with a novel xenograft rejection phenotype not seen in allotransplantation [78].

The field expanded beyond kidneys in 2025. The world’s first pig-to-human liver xenotransplant was performed at Xijing Hospital in China using a 6-gene-edited pig from ClonOrgan, producing golden bile within 2 hours of reperfusion [79]. A living patient received a pig auxiliary liver, surviving an unprecedented 171 days [80]. The first pig-to-human lung xenotransplant was reported in *Nature Medicine* [81]. Two competing platforms are advancing to formal trials: the EXPAND trial at NYU Langone using United Therapeutics’ 10-gene-edited UKidney, and eGenesis’ EGEN-2784 Phase 1/2/3 trial (FDA IND cleared September 2025) planning 33 patients over approximately 2.5 years [82]. These represent the first FDA-approved formal xenotransplantation clinical trials in history.

## 9. Synthetic Gene Circuits Implement Computational Logic for Precision Therapy

The ambition to embed Boolean logic, feedback control, and autonomous decision-making into therapeutic cells is moving from theoretical frameworks to functional demonstrations, with several approaches now entering clinical evaluation.

The most clinically advanced synthetic circuit is the Tmod NOT-gate system, where an activating CAR and a blocking CAR create a computation that destroys tumor cells bearing a target antigen only when they have lost HLA — a hallmark of many cancers through loss of heterozygosity. Phase 1 data from the EVEREST-2 trial confirmed tumor infiltration and no dose-limiting toxicities [30]. For preclinical cancer therapy, a Logic AND-Gate Dual-Target Genetic Circuit (LAG-DTGC) activates only when two cancer-specific biomarkers are simultaneously aberrantly expressed, achieving over 60% apoptosis induction in bladder cancer cells while sparing normal tissues and 70% tumor growth reduction in mouse models [83].

Feedback control systems — essential for sustained, autonomous therapeutic action — have reached new levels of sophistication. A CRISPR-based antithetic integral controller leveraging stoichiometric interaction between VPR-dCas9 and anti-CRISPR protein AcrIIA4 achieves robust perfect adaptation, maintaining constant protein expression across diverse perturbations including fluctuations in degradation rates and plasmid concentrations [84]. Computational frameworks for host-aware circuit design have demonstrated that post-transcriptional controllers generally outperform transcriptional ones for evolutionary longevity, and that growth-based feedback extends the functional half-life of therapeutic gene circuits — a critical requirement for chronic disease applications [85].

The Fussenegger laboratory’s comprehensive review in *Nature Reviews Genetics* (2026) maps the current state of programmable cell therapies, identifying three translational modalities: ex vivo autologous cell engineering (most mature), allogeneic off-the-shelf products (emerging), and in vivo reprogramming via mRNA nanoparticles and virus-like particles (frontier) [86]. A unified taxonomy of macromolecular switches at all gene expression levels — transcriptional (GPCR-based, melanopsin-based GEMS platforms), translational (riboswitches, toehold switches), and post-translational (degron systems including dTAG and SMASh, protease-based circuits) — establishes the engineering toolkit available for therapeutic circuit design [87]. The annual review of human synthetic biology provides the first comprehensive “parts list” for human gene expression control at DNA, RNA, and protein levels [88].

RNA-level circuits offer particular promise for gene therapy applications. SENTRs (Split-intron-Enabled Trans-Splicing Riboregulators) achieve digital logic computation with up to 6 inputs, low leakage expression, wide dynamic range, and machine-learning-predictable performance [89]. A high-resolution RNA-IN/RNA-OUT circuit using CRISPR-associated protease complexes amplifies single-nucleotide mutation signals from 1.5-fold to 94-fold, enabling dynamic monitoring of stem cell differentiation and rewiring of regulatory networks in mammalian cells [90]. Programmable anti-ribozymes that sense trigger RNAs for modulating gene expression represent another expanding modality for disease diagnosis and therapy [91].

## 10. CRISPR-Enhanced Phage Therapy and AI-Designed Antimicrobial Peptides Tackle Resistance

Antibiotic resistance kills an estimated 1.27 million people annually, and synthetic biology is delivering two fundamentally new weapon classes: CRISPR-enhanced bacteriophages and AI-designed antimicrobial peptides.

LBP-EC01 from Locus Biosciences is the most advanced CRISPR-phage therapeutic, consisting of a six-bacteriophage cocktail engineered with CRISPR-Cas3, which shreds target bacterial DNA rather than making precise cuts. In the Phase 2 ELIMINATE trial (NCT05488340), the first 39 patients with urinary tract infections showed rapid and durable *E. coli* reduction with symptom elimination, maximum urine drug concentrations of 6.3 × 10⁸ PFU/mL, and phage amplification in 9 of 11 evaluable patients — confirming in situ replication of the therapeutic. The cocktail covers approximately 95% of uropathogenic *E. coli* strains including multidrug-resistant variants. BARDA is co-funding up to $93 million of the $152 million program. Part 2 dosing is underway with up to 288 patients, and a second program (LBP-PA01) for *Pseudomonas* pneumonia has received NIH/NIAID funding for Phase 1b [92].

AI-driven antimicrobial peptide discovery has produced multiple candidates with in vivo efficacy against the most dangerous resistant pathogens. EvoGradient, an explainable deep learning model for virtual evolution of peptide sequences, identified peptides active against carbapenem-resistant *E. coli*, *K. pneumoniae*, *A. baumannii*, and vancomycin-resistant *Enterococcus*. The most potent peptide (pep-19-mod) achieved over 95% bacterial load reduction in mouse thigh infection models [93]. ProteoGPT, a generative protein large language model pipeline, produced AMPs with comparable or superior efficacy to clinical antibiotics in thigh infection models against CRAB and MRSA, with reduced resistance development and no organ damage or gut microbiota disruption [94]. Deep learning-guided de novo self-assembling peptides form nanofibrous structures on bacterial membranes, demonstrating therapeutic efficacy against acute intestinal infection and biofilm eradication [95]. These approaches are generating entirely new chemical matter that is orthogonal to existing antibiotic resistance mechanisms.

## 11. Optogenetic Vision Restoration Approaches Regulatory Approval

Optogenetics, the use of light-sensitive proteins to control cellular activity, has found its clinical beachhead in inherited retinal diseases, with multiple therapies advancing through trials and one approaching BLA submission.

Nanoscope Therapeutics’ MCO-010 is the most clinically advanced optogenetic therapy. The Multi-Characteristic Opsin (MCO-010) — a synthetic opsin delivered via optimized AAV2 targeting retinal bipolar cells — restores vision mutation-independently from a single intravitreal injection, responding to ambient visible light without requiring external goggles. Phase 1/2a data showed improvement in visual acuity, shape discrimination, and mobility in 4 blind retinitis pigmentosa (RP) patients over 52 weeks with no serious adverse events. The Phase 2b RESTORE trial for RP and Phase 2 STARLIGHT trial for Stargardt disease are both complete, with BLA submission planned for H1 2025 and FDA Fast Track plus Orphan Drug designations for both indications [96]. GenSight Biologics’ GS030, using the red-shifted channelrhodopsin ChrimsonR delivered via AAV2.7m8 paired with light-stimulating goggles, achieved partial visual recovery in a 58-year-old blind RP patient — the first reported case of optogenetic visual restoration in a human [97]. In total, 8 of 11 registered optogenetic clinical trials target retinal diseases.

A comprehensive roadmap published in *Nature Neuroscience* (2025) outlines two translational routes: direct deployment of optogenetic proteins for retinal therapy, and indirect use of optogenetic circuit mapping to guide drug development and neuromodulation for neuropsychiatric disorders [98]. Chemogenetics, while generating strong preclinical data — stable DREADD expression and functional efficacy for up to 2 years in nonhuman primates, and noninvasive seizure control via acoustically targeted chemogenetics (ATAC) achieving improvement in 93.3% of animals exceeding a transduction threshold — has not yet entered human clinical trials [99].

## 12. Regenerative Medicine Harnesses Synthetic Circuits and Engineered Living Materials

Synthetic biology is providing regenerative medicine with programmable control over tissue development, moving beyond simple stem cell differentiation toward designed multicellular systems.

The SYMPLE3D (SYnthetic Morphogen system for Pattern Logic Exploration using 3D spheroids) platform demonstrated that coupling synthetic morphogen signals with E-cadherin-based cell adhesion converts molecular gradients into distinct tissue domains with sharp boundaries — a critical capability for programming new tissue architectures in organoids [100]. This builds on foundational work converting inert proteins into synthetic morphogens that form gradients sensed by synNotch receptors, enabling creation of de novo multidomain tissue patterns [101]. Wendell Lim’s laboratory extended this approach by engineering synthetic organizer cells that guide tissue development using synNotch-based instruction programs [102] and synthetic suppressor T cells that execute locally targeted immunoprotective programs without systemic immunosuppression — with direct applications in transplant tolerance for islet cell protection [103].

3D bioprinting is integrating synthetic biology principles for functionally complex tissue constructs. The CHIPS/VAPOR platform (Collagen-based High-resolution Internally Perfusable Scaffolds integrated with Vascular and Perfusion Organ-on-a-chip Reactor) achieves ±20 µm resolution within centimeter-scale scaffolds, enabling bioprinting of beta-cell-like MIN6 with endothelial and stromal cells to create functional secretory tissue [104]. The integration of engineered living materials with 3D bioprinting harnesses the genetic programmability of encapsulated microorganisms through quorum sensing systems and genetic circuits for intercellular communication [105].

Clinical validation of synthetic biology-enhanced regenerative approaches is emerging. Stem cell-derived, fully differentiated islets for type 1 diabetes [106] and encapsulated allogeneic beta cells surviving without immunosuppression [107] demonstrate convergence between regenerative medicine and synthetic biology principles. Enzymatic conversion of blood group B kidneys for universal transplantation using engineered enzymes represents another frontier application [108].

## 13. Synthetic Chromosomes and Minimal Genomes Provide Foundational Infrastructure

The completion of the Synthetic Yeast Genome Project (Sc2.0) — a decade-long international effort — represents a watershed for synthetic genomics, providing design principles that will inform therapeutic applications of synthetic chromosomes.

In January 2025, construction of synXVI (902,994 bp), the final sixteenth synthetic *S. cerevisiae* chromosome, was completed, marking the conclusion of Sc2.0 and the creation of the first synthetic eukaryotic genome [109]. The project’s debugging insights — including the systematic identification of loxPsym sites near dubious ORFs disrupting 5’ UTRs of essential genes — provide blueprints for future genome-scale engineering. An international team has combined 6.5 synthetic chromosomes in a single functional cell [110]. For therapeutic applications, Human Artificial Chromosomes (HACs) using CENP-A nucleosome seeding bypass the need for repetitive centromeric DNA, with approximately 750 kb constructs efficiently forming single-copy HACs compatible with yeast spheroplast fusion delivery to mammalian cells [111]. Methods for high-fidelity human chromosome transfer and elimination have been demonstrated [112], and AI tools (AlphaGenome, Evo2) are being applied to genome design [113].

Work on minimal genomes continues to inform our understanding of essential biology. JCVI-syn3A (489 genes, 543 kbp) has been evolved for approximately 400 generations, with growth rate improvements exceeding 15% at the cost of 8 mutations per endpoint strain and a pronounced C/G→A/T transversion bias [114]. A comprehensive review of strategies for rational genome design toward fully synthetic cells was published in *Nature Reviews Bioengineering* (February 2026), mapping the path from current minimal organisms to bottom-up synthetic cells with potential therapeutic applications [115]. The emerging capability to synthesize and deploy large-scale DNA constructs — megabase-scale assembly, yeast-mediated transfer, and genome-scale rearrangement — represents infrastructure that will underpin future therapeutic synthetic biology.

-----

## 14. Clinical and Regulatory Milestones Signal Institutional Validation

The regulatory landscape has shifted decisively in favor of synthetic biology therapeutics, with multiple FDA approvals, breakthrough designations, and new policy frameworks that are emerging.

Casgevy (exa-cel), the CRISPR-Cas9 gene therapy for sickle cell disease and transfusion-dependent β-thalassemia, expanded to approval in the US, UK, EU, Switzerland, Canada, and multiple Middle Eastern countries by spring 2025, with 50 active treatment sites [116]. WU-CART-007 received FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designation in January 2026 for relapsed/refractory T-cell malignancies [32]. VERVE-102 and BEAM-302 both received FDA IND clearance in early 2025, with BEAM-302 aligned for potential accelerated approval [13, 14]. Neurogene NGN-401 for Rett syndrome, incorporating the EXACT gene regulation technology, received RMAT designation and FDA START Pilot Program acceptance [117]. The FDA approved the first formal clinical trials for xenotransplantation in February 2025 [82]. Lisocabtagene maraleucel (Breyanzi) received its seventh indication approval (marginal zone lymphoma) in December 2025, marking the seventh FDA-approved CAR-T product [118].

A significant regulatory shift occurred in December 2025: the FDA announced that future CAR-T approvals will generally require randomized controlled trials with standard-of-care comparators demonstrating superiority — all seven current approvals were based on single-arm trials without control groups [119]. This reflects the maturation of the field from experimental to established therapeutic modality. The global engineered living therapeutics market is projected to grow from $177.3 million in 2024 to $1.03 billion by 2032 (CAGR 24.6%), with CAR-T therapy revenues exceeding $5 billion in 2025 [120].

## Conclusion

This review reveals a field in transition. The fifteen topic areas surveyed share a common trajectory: from proof-of-concept toward clinical deployment, with AI acceleration compressing timelines at every stage.

Three overarching themes define the current landscape. First, AI has become the primary engine of biological design, not merely an optimization tool. AI-designed genome editors (OpenCRISPR-1), AI-designed proteins entering clinical trials (ABS-101), AI-designed mRNA sequences achieving 41-fold expression improvements (GEMORNA), and AI-designed antimicrobial peptides with in vivo efficacy represent a fundamental shift from discovery to engineering in biological therapeutics. Second, the gap between in vivo and ex vivo approaches is narrowing. In vivo base editing (BEAM-302, VERVE-102), in vivo epigenome editing (CHARM), in vivo microbiome editing (BACTRINS, MetaEdit), and in vivo CAR generation via LNP-delivered mRNA collectively suggest a future where therapeutic genetic modification does not require removing cells from the body. Third, synthetic biology circuits are implementing genuine computational logic in therapeutic contexts — NOT gates protecting normal tissues from CAR-T toxicity, fuzzy logic discriminating tumor from healthy cells, integral feedback controllers maintaining homeostatic protein levels, and quorum sensing switches controlling bacterial therapeutic activation in tumors.

The most significant gap remains between preclinical promise and clinical validation. Despite hundreds of elegant circuit designs in cell lines and mouse models, the vast majority of clinical successes to date involve relatively simple genetic modifications — gene knockouts, single-gene insertions, or constitutive expression constructs. The translation of complex multi-input logic gates, feedback controllers, and autonomous decision-making circuits into approved therapeutics represents the next frontier. Delivery, immunogenicity, evolutionary stability of synthetic circuits, and manufacturing scalability remain persistent bottlenecks. The field would benefit from standardized metrics for comparing therapeutic circuits, long-term follow-up studies for gene-edited therapies, and systematic investigation of how circuit complexity interacts with clinical safety and efficacy.

Nevertheless, the evidence compiled here supports a strong conclusion: synthetic biology is no longer a technology searching for applications. It is a therapeutic modality with multiple approved products, dozens of clinical programs, and a research trajectory suggesting that the most transformative applications — closed-loop therapeutic circuits, programmable tissue engineering, AI-designed biologics, and universal organ replacement — are not decades away but years away from clinical reality.
