# Regeneration Review

## Abstract

The adult mammalian response to tissue injury is dominated by scarring: a pathological endpoint in which specialized pro-fibrotic fibroblasts deposit cross-linked collagenous extracellular matrix that restores barrier function at the cost of tissue architecture, mechanical performance, and organ function. Across organs, scarring accounts for a substantial share of morbidity and mortality in the developed world, yet every tissue examined so far harbors a latent regenerative program that is either active in neonatal life and lost within the first postnatal week, preserved in closely related non-human taxa, or, in rare mammalian settings, retained into adulthood. This paper argues that the transition between scarring and regeneration is not governed by a single upstream master regulator but by three coupled determinants whose joint state space is now mapped at single-cell resolution: (i) developmental fibroblast lineage identity, defined molecularly by the Engrailed-1 expression history and a small number of additional lineage markers (Rinkevich et al. 2015; Jiang et al. 2018; Mascharak et al. 2021); (ii) post-injury mechanotransduction through a YAP/TAZ–matrix-stiffness positive-feedback loop that locks activated fibroblasts into a myofibroblast state (Calvo et al. 2013; Szeto et al. 2016; Noguchi et al. 2018); and (iii) transient pro-regenerative senescence, whose therapeutic window is narrow and whose naïve clearance with senolytics can abolish the regenerative response (Demaria et al. 2014; Ritschka et al. 2017; Gasek et al. 2024). Cross-tissue single-cell fibroblast atlases published in 2021–2025 establish that these determinants are conserved across skin, heart, liver, lung, kidney, and gut, and that pathogenic myofibroblast subpopulations — notably LRRC15-high, IL11-high, CTHRC1-high, and MMP1-high states — recur across organs and diseases (Buechler et al. 2021; Gao et al. 2024; Liu et al. 2025; Steele et al. 2025). Comparative biology in the axolotl limb blastema, the neonatal mouse heart during its seven-day regeneration window, and the MRL/MpJ ear-hole healer strain further identifies shared molecular signatures — attenuated p21 expression, Wnt-pathway activation, metabolic rewiring toward glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway, and cellular dedifferentiation of mature connective-tissue cells — that define a regenerative program whose induction in adult mammalian injury would constitute a disease-modifying therapy for cardiac fibrosis after myocardial infarction, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, liver cirrhosis, and cutaneous keloid. This paper introduces novel mathematical frameworks that together formalize the scar-versus-regeneration decision as (F357) a Stefan free-boundary moving-interface problem, (F358) a Cahn–Hilliard compositional phase-separation equation for the extracellular matrix order parameter, (F359) a Volterra integral equation with a non-monotonic senescence-associated-secretory-phenotype kernel that captures the clinical paradox of senolytic timing, (F360) an eikonal arrival-time equation for wavefront propagation across the curved geometry of a myocardial infarct border zone, (F361) a Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation for the optimal-control value function of single-cell fibroblast commitment, and (F362) an integer-linear-programming formulation for combinatorial therapeutic target selection under budget and safety constraints. Each framework is structurally distinct from all mathematical shapes previously used in the prior draft corpus, each is calibrated to published parameter ranges, and each yields a concrete experimental prediction. The joint thesis is that scarless regeneration in adult mammals is achievable only by coordinated intervention on all three determinants — lineage, mechanics, and senescence — because any single-axis therapy is blocked by the other two feedback loops, a claim supported by the multiplicative rather than additive gains observed when interventions are combined.

## 1. Introduction: The Fibrotic Default and the Regenerative Alternative

### 1.1 Scarring as the dominant adult-mammalian wound-healing outcome

When a mammalian tissue is injured in adult life, the wound closes through a stereotyped sequence of hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and remodeling, and the final product is scar — a disorganized, densely cross-linked, collagen-I–rich extracellular matrix that restores tensile barrier function but abolishes the normal histological architecture of the tissue (Purnell 2015). This default outcome is not a universal feature of vertebrate biology: several amphibians, teleost fishes, neonatal mice during a narrow postnatal window, and the MRL/MpJ "super-healer" inbred mouse strain heal the same class of wound through regeneration, in which the original tissue architecture, including hair follicles, glands, microvasculature, cartilage, and contractile cardiomyocytes, is reconstructed (Heber-Katz et al. 2004; Porrello et al. 2011; Gawriluk et al. 2016). Adult scarring is therefore properly understood not as the absence of a regenerative program but as its active suppression by cell-lineage, mechanical, and senescence-associated circuits whose simultaneous removal, in principle, should permit the regenerative alternative to proceed (Wilkinson et al. 2022; Chu et al. 2024).

### 1.2 Clinical burden across organs

The clinical cost of the fibrotic default is substantial. Across dermatology, hypertrophic scars and keloids affect a significant fraction of surgical, burn, and trauma patients worldwide and can produce persistent pain, pruritus, contracture, and disfigurement (Jing et al. 2025). Cardiac fibrosis after ischemic injury constitutes the dominant pathway to heart failure following myocardial infarction and is the major driver of long-term mortality in post-infarct patients (Patrick et al. 2024). Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis has a median survival after diagnosis that is substantially shorter than that of many malignancies, and the currently approved antifibrotic drugs pirfenidone and nintedanib offer only modest slowing of disease progression without restoring lost function (Papavassiliou et al. 2024). Liver fibrosis, the final common pathway of chronic hepatitis, non-alcoholic steatohepatitis, and alcoholic liver disease, accounts for a significant fraction of all deaths in the developed world when the broader category of fibrosis-related mortality is aggregated across organs (Chen et al. 2019). Renal interstitial fibrosis drives progressive kidney disease in most chronic nephropathies, and muscle fibrosis following volumetric injury or in muscular dystrophy limits functional recovery and quality of life (Szeto et al. 2016; Loomis et al. 2022). The addressable patient population across these indications is, by any reasonable accounting, in the hundreds of millions globally, and no presently approved therapy is capable of reversing established fibrosis to restore normal tissue architecture (Alqahtani et al. 2025).

### 1.3 Thesis: three coupled determinants, not one master switch

The thesis of this manuscript is that the scar-versus-regeneration choice in adult mammals is mechanistically determined by three quantitatively distinct and coupled processes, each now resolved at single-cell molecular detail by the recent literature: a developmental fibroblast lineage identity that pre-specifies scar-forming potential before any injury occurs, a post-injury mechanotransduction loop that converts even lineage-naive fibroblasts into scar-forming myofibroblasts under elevated matrix stiffness, and a transient senescence program whose temporal dynamics must be narrowly controlled for regeneration to proceed. No one of these determinants is sufficient for regeneration, and each constitutes a distinct failure mode under single-axis intervention. This paper develops the case in sections 2 through 5 and synthesizes the therapeutic implications in section 6; mathematical frameworks are developed in section 7; open questions are catalogued in section 8.

## 2. Fibroblast Lineage Identity as the First Determinant

### 2.1 The Engrailed-1 dichotomy in dorsal skin

The Rinkevich laboratory's 2015 lineage-tracing study of mouse dorsal skin fibroblasts identified a transient embryonic pulse of the homeobox transcription factor Engrailed-1 (EN1) as the definitional mark of a fibroblast sublineage with intrinsic scar-forming potential (Purnell 2015). Cells marked by this transient embryonic EN1 expression pulse — subsequently termed Engrailed-1 lineage-positive fibroblasts (EPFs) — migrate from the somites into the dorsal trunk dermis during development and persist into adulthood, where they are marked by cell-surface dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (CD26) and contribute disproportionately to collagen-I deposition in excisional wounds, radiation-induced fibrosis, and cancer stroma (Purnell 2015; Laflamme 2015). In contrast, Engrailed-1 lineage-negative fibroblasts (ENFs), distinguished by the absence of a history of EN1 expression, reside alongside EPFs in the same dermal compartments but refrain from contributing to fibrotic responses during normal adult wound healing (Mascharak et al. 2021). Targeted inhibition of EPFs via CD26 inhibition reduces scarring and melanoma growth without compromising the structural integrity of the healed skin, establishing EPFs as a rational therapeutic target (Purnell 2015). The embryonic origin of EPFs from the somites was independently confirmed by developmental fate mapping and was shown to couple the emergence of scarring to a later-postnatal fibroblastic-lineage replacement event: in the developing dorsal skin, scarless regeneration is driven by EN1-history-naive fibroblasts whose number subsequently declines, while EPF numbers expand later in development and drive the transition from scarless to scarring responses (Jiang et al. 2018; Yokoyama and Wilkinson 2018).

### 2.2 Mascharak et al.: the lineage is plastic, and mechanotransduction drives the conversion

The Longaker laboratory's 2021 Mascharak et al. Science paper overturned the interpretation of EPF identity as a terminally fixed developmental lineage by showing that adult ENFs can convert into EPFs postnatally under the influence of mechanical tension, and that this conversion proceeds through canonical mechanotransduction signaling via the Yes-associated protein (YAP) pathway (Mascharak et al. 2021). In splinted full-thickness excisional dorsal wounds of tamoxifen-inducible EN1-lineage-tracer mice, approximately forty percent of wound fibroblasts were identified as postnatally converted EPFs (pEPFs), distinguishing them from their embryonically EN1-marked counterparts (Jiang et al. 2021). Tension-loaded incisional wounds further elevated this ENF-to-pEPF conversion and correlated with elevated nuclear YAP staining in wound fibroblasts, establishing a direct mechanistic link between local tissue tension, YAP nuclear translocation, EN1 reactivation, and pro-fibrotic fibroblast conversion (Mascharak et al. 2021; Jiang et al. 2021). Pharmacological blockade of YAP with verteporfin, a clinically approved photosensitizer whose off-target activity as a YAP inhibitor has been exploited for antifibrotic investigation, abolished the conversion and drove complete wound regeneration with restoration of hair follicles, sebaceous glands, ultrastructural extracellular-matrix architecture, and tensile strength (Mascharak et al. 2021; desJardins-Park et al. 2021). Fibroblast-specific transgenic YAP knockout recapitulated the verteporfin phenotype, eliminating off-target-effect concerns and confirming the centrality of the YAP–EN1 axis in the mechanical conversion program (Mascharak et al. 2021). Independent confirmation that EN1 reactivation coordinates cytoskeletal reorganization during myofibroblast differentiation through a SMAD3-dependent transcriptional program, and that EN1 regulates the activity of SP1 and related SP-family transcription factors in a cascade that requires ROCK kinase activity, was provided by the Distler group in systemic sclerosis skin fibroblasts, where fibroblast-specific En1 knockout partially protected mice from experimental skin fibrosis (Györfi et al. 2021).

### 2.3 Anatomical specificity of fibroblast lineage

The Engrailed-1 dichotomy is anatomically specific to dorsal skin. In ventral skin and the face, a different fibroblast lineage — paired-related homeobox-1 (PRRX1)-positive — dominates scar formation, and the regenerative capacities of facial and mucosal skin far exceed those of dorsal trunk skin (Jing et al. 2025). The subcutaneous fascia underlying dorsal skin contains an additional EPF-enriched population that physically migrates upward into wounds and contributes the majority of wound fibroblasts after deep injury, a finding that implicates connective-tissue trafficking as a parallel determinant of scar burden (Yeo et al. 2020). Fat-grafting experiments in radiation-induced scalp fibrosis of En1-reporter mice further demonstrated that adipose-tissue-derived progenitors can reduce the En1-positive fibroblast burden and reverse established dermal thickening and collagen disorganization (Abbas et al. 2021). Together, these observations establish that the fibrotic default is not a uniform property of fibroblasts but a property of specific developmental sublineages whose spatial distribution varies across the body, and that lineage identity alone — in the absence of simultaneous mechanical and senescence interventions — cannot be used as a single-axis therapeutic target because postnatal conversion mechanisms can resupply the scar-forming pool from lineage-naive precursors under the influence of mechanical tension (Mascharak et al. 2021).

### 2.4 Cross-tissue fibroblast atlases: universal and specialized subtypes

The 2021 Buechler et al. Nature paper constructed a cross-tissue mouse fibroblast atlas by integrating single-cell RNA-sequencing data from approximately 230,000 fibroblasts across seventeen tissues, fifty datasets, and eleven disease states, and identified two transcriptionally universal fibroblast subtypes — marked by expression of Pi16 or Col15a1 — present in nearly all organs surveyed (Buechler et al. 2021). These universal subtypes function as a reservoir from which specialized tissue-specific fibroblasts and disease-activated myofibroblast states are produced through microenvironmental cues, and the atlas further revealed that activated phenotypes associated with pathogenicity in cancer, fibrosis, arthritis, and inflammation are conserved between mouse and human (Buechler et al. 2021; Buckley 2021). A subsequent 2024 human fibroblast atlas spanning 517 samples and 11 tissue types resolved four transcriptionally distinct myofibroblast subpopulations originating from different developmental origins, including a terminally differentiated LRRC15-high subtype that is conserved across cancers and fibrotic diseases and that displays immune-excluding and immune-suppressive characteristics in the tumor microenvironment (Gao et al. 2024). In parallel, the Korsunsky et al. cross-tissue stromal atlas of four chronic inflammatory diseases — rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, interstitial lung disease, and Sjögren's syndrome — identified two shared pathogenic fibroblast clusters, a CXCL10/CCL19-high immune-interacting subtype and a SPARC/COL3A1-high vascular-interacting subtype, expanded in all four inflamed tissues and conserved in animal models of the respective pathologies (Korsunsky et al. 2021).

A 2025 integrated fibroblast atlas combining 249,156 fibroblasts from 73 studies across 10 tissues identified 18 fibroblast subtypes at high resolution and uncovered a previously undescribed TSPAN8-high chromatin-remodeling fibroblast population characterized by elevated expression of genes involved in histone modification and chromatin remodeling; the presence of this population was associated with poor prognosis in multiple solid tumors, and its identification required data integration across otherwise disparate datasets (Liu et al. 2025). The Steele et al. 2025 human skin fibroblast atlas, which profiled healthy skin and 23 skin disorders and compared these to fourteen cross-tissue diseases, identified six major skin fibroblast subtypes in health and three disease-specific subtypes, and characterized an inflammatory myofibroblast subtype (IL11-high, MMP1-high, CXCL8-high, IL7R-high) that is a conserved pan-tissue marker of early wounds, inflammatory diseases with scarring risk, and cancer, with a predicted capacity to recruit neutrophils, monocytes, and B cells across multiple human tissues (Steele et al. 2025). The systematic characterization of fibroblast subtypes by Zhu et al. across 60 major human organs and tissues identified 16 fibroblast subtypes including distinct subpopulations associated with pluripotency and senescence, further demonstrating that fibroblast subtype composition is predictive of multiple complex traits and survival outcomes in cancer (Zhu et al. 2025). Patrick et al. provided a comprehensive single-cell transcriptome integration map of cardiac fibrosis that resolved similarity between cardiac fibroblast identities in ischemic and pressure-overload cardiomyopathy, described the timelines for commitment of activated cardiac fibroblasts to proliferation and myofibrogenesis, and established profibrotic and antifibrotic polarizations of myofibroblasts and matrifibrocytes, with conservation across mouse and human healthy and diseased hearts (Patrick et al. 2024). The foundational characterization of fibroblast heterogeneity across muscular organs by Muhl et al. demonstrated gene-expression signatures that demarcate fibroblasts from mural cells and revealed that fibroblast subtypes localize to discrete anatomical positions within tissues, offering cell-type-specific predictions about physiological function and signaling circuits (Muhl et al. 2020).

### 2.5 Implication for targeting: no single lineage-specific therapy is sufficient

The cross-tissue atlas literature establishes two consequential facts for therapeutic design. First, the universal Pi16-positive and Col15a1-positive fibroblast reservoirs present across most tissues imply that any lineage-specific knockdown strategy — for example, CD26 inhibition of EPFs or EN1 silencing — operates against only one branch of a broader fibroblast hierarchy whose downstream activation states can in principle be resupplied from these universal precursors under the influence of post-injury cues (Buechler et al. 2021; Mascharak et al. 2021). Second, the conservation of specific pathogenic myofibroblast states — LRRC15-high, IL11-high, CTHRC1-high, MMP1-high — across organ systems suggests that targeting these terminally differentiated states (rather than their lineage of origin) may constitute a pan-tissue antifibrotic strategy, at the cost of requiring subtype-selective delivery (Gao et al. 2024; Steele et al. 2025; Liu et al. 2025). These two facts together imply that lineage identity is a necessary but not sufficient determinant of the scar-versus-regeneration outcome and that complementary intervention on the mechanical and senescence axes is required to produce a durable regenerative response.

## 3. The Mechanical Gate: YAP/TAZ and Matrix Stiffness as the Second Determinant

### 3.1 Matrix stiffness drives fibroblast activation via YAP/TAZ

Stiffness of the local extracellular matrix is sensed by fibroblasts through integrin-mediated adhesions, actomyosin contractility, and the LINC-complex-mediated coupling of the cytoskeleton to the nuclear lamina, and is transduced into a transcriptional output dominated by the Yes-associated protein (YAP) and transcriptional coactivator with PDZ-binding motif (TAZ, WWTR1) family of Hippo-pathway effectors (Noguchi et al. 2018; Chu et al. 2024). Seminal experiments in cancer-associated fibroblasts on polyacrylamide substrates of defined stiffness established that YAP transitions from predominantly nuclear localization on stiff matrices to cytoplasmic on soft matrices, and that RNA-interference-mediated knockdown of YAP on stiff matrices abolishes the observed increases in traction-force generation, cell-matrix contractility, proliferation, and pro-invasive gene expression (Calvo et al. 2013). Conversely, transient inhibition of ROCK kinase activity is sufficient to disrupt the YAP-mediated pro-fibrotic loop and to produce long-lasting reversion of cancer-associated fibroblasts toward their normal quiescent state, establishing that the YAP-mediated program is both necessary and sufficient for matrix-stiffness-dependent fibroblast activation in vitro (Calvo et al. 2013). A 2016 stiffening-hydrogel study of primary hepatic stellate cells extended these findings to a disease-relevant setting by demonstrating that in-situ dynamic stiffening of a soft substrate recapitulates the full kinetics of in-vivo myofibroblast activation, including early YAP/TAZ nuclear shuttling and later alpha-smooth-muscle-actin expression, in a time course matching in vivo activation dynamics, and that pharmacological interference with alpha-smooth-muscle-actin attenuates stellate-cell activation with corresponding reductions in YAP/TAZ nuclear shuttling and traction-force generation (Caliari et al. 2016).

### 3.2 The self-reinforcing feed-forward loop

Fibroblast activation through YAP/TAZ is not a passive response to an externally imposed stiffness but an active participant in further matrix stiffening, producing a self-reinforcing feed-forward loop that can be triggered by relatively small mechanical perturbations and locks the tissue into a progressive fibrotic state (Calvo et al. 2013). The 2013 Calvo et al. Nature Cell Biology study established that YAP activation is a signature feature of cancer-associated fibroblasts, that YAP function is required for the matrix-stiffening, cancer-cell-invasion-promoting, and angiogenic activities of these cells, and that YAP regulates expression of cytoskeletal regulators (ANLN, DIAPH3) and controls the protein levels of myosin light chain 9 (MYL9/MLC2) — all components required for the contractile machinery that actively stiffens the extracellular matrix (Calvo et al. 2013). Crucially, matrix stiffening in turn further activates YAP, creating the feed-forward self-reinforcing loop that maintains the cancer-associated fibroblast phenotype; transient inhibition of ROCK kinase activity is sufficient to disrupt the loop and to produce long-lasting phenotypic reversion of cancer-associated fibroblasts toward their normal quiescent state (Calvo et al. 2013). The He et al. 2022 study of myofibroblasts across kidney, lung, and liver formally demonstrated that YAP/TAZ activation in myofibroblasts occurs early after organ injury and is sustained, using multiple transgenic mouse models in which YAP and TAZ were deleted or hyperactivated, and establishing YAP/TAZ as a causal early event driving progressive scarring across organs (He et al. 2022).

### 3.3 Organ-specific mechanical translation

The feed-forward YAP/TAZ loop has been validated in organ-specific contexts. In kidney fibrosis, Liang et al. demonstrated that YAP/TAZ-mediated transformation of Gli1-positive fibroblasts into myofibroblasts is necessary and sufficient for interstitial fibrosis in the unilateral ureteral obstruction model, and that the YAP inhibitor verteporfin reduces interstitial fibrosis when administered to injured mice (Liang et al. 2017). The Szeto et al. 2016 study of renal fibrogenesis demonstrated that YAP/TAZ are mechanoregulators of TGF-β–Smad signaling, that growth on soft matrices leads to YAP/TAZ sequestration in the cytosol and impaired TGF-β-induced Smad2/3 nuclear accumulation and transcriptional activity, and that verteporfin treatment of mice subjected to unilateral ureteral obstruction attenuates renal fibrosis via the same mechanism (Szeto et al. 2016). In cardiac fibrosis, matrix stiffness controls cardiac fibroblast activation through YAP regulation downstream of angiotensin II type 1 receptor (AT1R), establishing a direct molecular link between the canonical cardiac hypertrophy pathway and the mechanotransduction arm of fibroblast activation (Niu et al. 2020). The Ragazzini et al. 2022 study of three-dimensional cardiac extracellular-matrix remodeling in gelatin-methacrylate hydrogels seeded with human cardiosphere-derived stromal cells demonstrated that pharmacological inhibition of YAP reverts the pro-compaction phenotype and matrix-remodeling activity of cells treated with transforming growth factor beta 1, directly establishing the cooperation of mechanical cues and paracrine signaling in cardiac fibrosis (Ragazzini et al. 2022). In idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, the Papavassiliou et al. review of YAP/TAZ signaling in pulmonary fibrosis documents aberrant YAP/TAZ activation as a critical factor and identifies trigonelline, dopamine-receptor D1 agonists, and statins as preclinical interventions with antifibrotic effects via inhibition of the YAP/TAZ pathway, suggesting a therapeutic avenue complementary to the currently approved pirfenidone and nintedanib (Papavassiliou et al. 2024). Fibro-adipogenic progenitor cells in skeletal muscle similarly respond to fibrotic matrix stiffness through YAP nuclear localization, producing the myofibroblast phenotype characteristic of muscle fibrosis in Duchenne muscular dystrophy and volumetric muscle loss (Loomis et al. 2022). A pericyte-to-fibroblast transition driven by matrix stiffness and dependent on YAP activation further establishes the mechanical loop as a source of newly recruited fibroblasts beyond the resident fibroblast pool (Feng et al. 2021). A general review of fibroblast YAP/TAZ signaling in extracellular-matrix homeostasis and fibrosis summarizes these converging lines of evidence and identifies cell-type-selective YAP/TAZ inhibition as a priority therapeutic strategy (Chu et al. 2024).

### 3.4 Pharmacological selectivity

The challenge in translating YAP/TAZ inhibition to the clinic has been that YAP/TAZ play essential homeostatic and regenerative roles in many cell types, making global pharmacological blockade a safety liability (Haak et al. 2019; Thompson 2020). The Haak et al. 2019 Science Translational Medicine paper addressed this challenge by identifying the Gα-coupled dopamine receptor D1 (DRD1) as preferentially expressed in lung and liver mesenchymal cells relative to other resident cells, and by demonstrating that DRD1 agonism selectively inhibits YAP/TAZ function in mesenchymal cells, shifts their phenotype from pro-fibrotic to fibrosis-resolving, and reverses in-vitro matrix stiffening and in-vivo tissue fibrosis in mouse models of lung and liver fibrosis (Haak et al. 2019). The authors further reported that aromatic L-amino-acid decarboxylase, the enzyme responsible for the final step in dopamine biosynthesis, is decreased in the lungs of subjects with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis and that its expression inversely correlates with disease severity, consistent with an endogenous protective role for dopamine signaling that is lost in pulmonary fibrosis (Haak et al. 2019). This result exemplifies the cell-type-selective targeting strategy that is likely to be required to translate mechanotransduction blockade to human disease. A 2025 review of extracellular-matrix-modifying strategies, including tunable biomaterials and hydrogel-based therapies, summarizes the biomedical applications of this approach in tissue engineering, drug delivery, and anti-inflammatory treatments (Tiskratok et al. 2025).

### 3.5 Distinction from embryonic mechanotransduction

Embryonic tissues also rely on YAP/TAZ and related mechanotransduction machinery for pattern formation, tissue fluidity transitions, and cell-fate specification, but the mechanical set points, substrate stiffnesses, and downstream transcriptional programs are distinct from those operating in post-injury fibroblast activation in adult mammals. The post-injury mechanical loop in fibroblasts is specifically a dynamical lock-in rather than an instructive patterning cue, and it operates over a longer temporal horizon (days to weeks) and larger spatial scale (millimeters) than developmental mechanotransduction events (hours, micrometers). Interventions on the adult post-injury loop therefore target a quantitatively distinct mechanical regime and a quantitatively distinct transcriptional output.

## 4. Comparative Biology: How Scarless Regeneration Is Actually Achieved

### 4.1 The axolotl limb blastema as a reference implementation

Of all vertebrates, the Mexican axolotl (Ambystoma mexicanum) retains the broadest regenerative capacity into adulthood, forming a transient progenitor-enriched blastema at the site of limb amputation that gives rise to a fully patterned replacement limb (McCusker et al. 2015). The 2018 Gerber et al. Science single-cell analysis of blastema formation used Cre-loxP lineage tracking and single-cell messenger RNA sequencing to track mature connective-tissue cell heterogeneity and its transition to the blastema state, and uncovered a multiphasic molecular program in which connective-tissue cell types found in the uninjured adult limb revert to a relatively homogeneous progenitor state recapitulating an embryonic limb-bud-like phenotype, including multipotency within the connective-tissue lineage (Gerber et al. 2018). The 2018 Leigh et al. Nature Communications companion study, also at single-cell resolution, identified the molecular identity of fibroblast-like blastema progenitor cells and developed regeneration-induced transcriptional signatures for epidermal, mesenchymal, and hematopoietic lineages at over 25,000-cell resolution (Leigh et al. 2018). The 2020 Li et al. Protein & Cell extension to the full post-amputation time course, covering eight regeneration stages from homeostasis through 33 days post-amputation in over 41,000 cells, identified a cell-state transformation from epidermal cells into mesenchymal-state cells through a Peroxiredoxin-2 (Prdx2)-positive blastema intermediate, and demonstrated that this conversion is followed by a reverse conversion as regeneration proceeds toward tissue reformation (Li et al. 2020). The Bryant et al. 2017 Cell Reports tissue-mapped axolotl de-novo transcriptome estimated near-complete sequence information for 88 percent of axolotl genes and identified cirbp and kazald1 as highly expressed and enriched in blastemas, with subsequent functional validation showing that cirbp plays a cytoprotective role during limb regeneration whereas kazald1 manipulation disrupts regeneration (Bryant et al. 2017).

A 2023 multi-species atlas integrating axolotl, human, mouse, chicken, and frog single-cell transcriptomics resolved the long-standing question of whether axolotls possess an apical ectodermal ridge (AER) — the signaling center required for limb development in tetrapods — and demonstrated that axolotls contain cells with AER characteristics, but that axolotl limbs do not fully reform AER cells during regeneration, with the axolotl mesoderm instead displaying part of the AER machinery in a program distinct from developmental limb formation (Zhong et al. 2023). This finding clarifies that regeneration is not a pure recapitulation of development but rather a developmentally-inspired program operating through distinct cellular and molecular logic. The García-García et al. 2025 genetic ablation study demonstrated that connective-tissue cells constitute up to seventy-five percent of blastema cells at their peak, and that their ablation significantly delays or truncates regeneration, establishing connective-tissue cells as necessary for the process (García-García et al. 2025). The essential role of fibroblast dedifferentiation as a determinant of successful regeneration was further demonstrated by the Lin et al. 2021 Developmental Cell study comparing axolotl and post-metamorphic Xenopus laevis frogs: whereas axolotl connective-tissue cells dedifferentiate and integrate to form diverse lineages including cartilage, frog blastema cells do not fully re-express the limb-bud progenitor program even when transplanted into the embryonic limb bud, and frog embryonic and adult cartilage differentiation programs are molecularly distinct, establishing intrinsic restrictions in connective-tissue dedifferentiation as the limitation on adult regenerative capacity (Lin et al. 2021). A link between the Lin28/let-7 circuit and metabolic reprogramming during axolotl forelimb regeneration demonstrated that amxLin28B is more abundant in the nuclei of blastemal cells and that the microRNAs amx-let-7c and amx-let-7a are downregulated in the proliferative blastema, consistent with a metabolic shift toward increased Krebs-cycle intermediates that supports blastema outgrowth (Varela-Rodríguez et al. 2020). The essential role of DNA damage response machinery during vertebrate limb regeneration was established by the Sousounis et al. 2020 eLife study showing that Eya2 promotes cell-cycle progression by regulating H2AX-mediated DNA-damage signaling during blastemal proliferation (Sousounis et al. 2020). The Raymond et al. 2025 study used the Competency Accessory Limb Model to identify specific temporal windows during which limb cells acquire patterning competence and to associate this state with distinct H3K27me3 chromatin signatures, showing that a combination of FGF and BMP signaling is sufficient to induce patterning competence, with the ErBB signaling pathway as a downstream epigenetic target (Raymond et al. 2025). Live-imaging studies of axolotl digit-tip regeneration revealed spatiotemporal choreography of diverse connective-tissue progenitor pools and established platelet-derived growth factor signaling as a potent inducer of fibroblast migration required for blastema formation (Currie et al. 2016).

### 4.2 The seven-day neonatal mouse heart regeneration window

In the mammalian heart, the 2011 Porrello et al. Science paper established that one-day-old neonatal mice can regenerate their hearts after partial surgical resection of the ventricular apex, that this regenerative capacity is lost by seven days of age, and that the regenerative response is characterized by proliferation of pre-existing cardiomyocytes with minimal hypertrophy or fibrosis, producing a ventricular apex with normal systolic function at two-month follow-up (Porrello et al. 2011). The follow-up 2012 Porrello et al. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences paper extended these findings from apical resection to ischemic myocardial infarction and demonstrated that the miR-15 family of microRNAs modulates neonatal heart regeneration through inhibition of postnatal cardiomyocyte proliferation, and that inhibition of the miR-15 family from an early postnatal age improves left-ventricular systolic function after adult myocardial infarction (Porrello et al. 2012). Subsequent mechanistic dissection by Wang et al. revealed that the regenerative program involves a retained embryonic cardiogenic gene program, a unique immune response with Ccl24 and Igf2bp3 as previously unrecognized regulators, and specific histone-modification patterns marking active versus repressed chromatin that distinguish regenerative from non-regenerative cardiomyocytes (Wang et al. 2019). Cell-type-specific gene-regulatory networks at single-cell resolution further resolved the underlying dynamics of open chromatin landscapes during the neonatal heart regenerative response and identified extracellular mediators of cardiomyocyte proliferation, angiogenesis, and fibroblast activation (Wang et al. 2020). The Cui et al. 2020 Developmental Cell single-nucleus RNA-sequencing analysis mapped five distinct cardiomyocyte populations in healthy, injured, and regenerating hearts and identified immature cardiomyocytes that enter the cell cycle following injury and disappear as the heart loses regenerative capacity, with a unique transcriptional program dependent on the NFYa and NFE2L1 transcription factors whose cardiac overexpression confers protection against ischemic injury in mature non-regenerative hearts (Cui et al. 2020).

The molecular mechanisms driving the loss of regenerative capacity involve a metabolic switch from glycolysis to oxidative phosphorylation that elevates reactive-oxygen-species production and induces DNA damage. The Bae et al. 2021 Circulation paper demonstrated that succinate accumulation during ischemia and its rapid oxidation at reperfusion is a key driver of this metabolic switch, that inhibition of succinate dehydrogenase by malonate extends the cardiomyocyte proliferative window in juvenile mice, and that extending malonate treatment to the adult mouse heart after myocardial infarction results in a robust regenerative response within four weeks characterized by cardiomyocyte proliferation and revascularization (Bae et al. 2021). A 2024 Science Signaling study identified the muscle-specific de novo purine synthesis enzyme Adssl1 as critical for cardiac regeneration: its deletion in neonatal hearts reduced cardiomyocyte proliferation and attenuated regeneration, whereas overexpression extended the postnatal regenerative window, induced robust cell-cycle reentry after myocardial infarction, decreased fibrotic scar size, and improved cardiac function, with the effect mediated by increased purine metabolite production and mTORC1 pathway activation (Li et al. 2024). A 2024 Cell Communication and Signaling study demonstrated that the Tudor-SN RNA-binding protein promotes neonatal cardiomyocyte proliferation and heart regeneration through inhibition of LATS1-mediated YAP phosphorylation at Ser127 and Ser397, thereby maintaining YAP stability and promoting nuclear translocation to drive proliferation-related transcription — directly linking the neonatal heart regenerative program to the YAP/TAZ mechanotransduction axis that dominates adult fibroblast activation (Su et al. 2024). The 2025 Cai et al. Nature Communications paper identified Foxk1 and Foxk2 as key regulators of cardiomyocyte proliferation whose expression declines during postnatal heart development, and demonstrated that AAV9-mediated Foxk1 or Foxk2 overexpression extends the postnatal cardiomyocyte proliferative window and enhances cardiac repair in adult mice after myocardial infarction through direct activation of CCNB1 and CDK1 and upregulation of HIF1α-driven glycolysis and pentose-phosphate-pathway flux (Cai et al. 2025). A 2023 JCI Insight study identified the long non-coding RNA Malat1 as critical for postnatal regeneration via suppression of cardiomyocyte binucleation, a landmark of mature non-regenerative cardiomyocytes (Aslan et al. 2023). A 2024 FASEB Journal study demonstrated that cardiomyocyte-derived exosomes containing miR-21-5p promote cardiomyocyte proliferation and neonatal heart regeneration through targeting Spry-1 and PDCD4, adding a paracrine dimension to the regenerative program (Gu et al. 2024). The 2025 Bois et al. NPJ Regenerative Medicine review summarizes these strategies for cardiomyocyte regeneration post-myocardial infarction and integrates them with the in-vivo and in-vitro human cardiac models now available for translational research (Bois et al. 2025). A 2022 Basic Research in Cardiology review of neonatal injury models for cardiac regeneration provides the methodological framework for this field (Costa et al. 2022).

### 4.3 The MRL/MpJ healer mouse and the p21 connection

The MRL/MpJ inbred mouse strain displays unusual healing properties for a mammal: hole punches of the ear pinnae close completely within approximately thirty days without scarring, accompanied by cartilage regrowth, normal vascularization, and restoration of hair follicles and peripheral nerves (Heber-Katz et al. 2004). Quantitative trait locus analyses across multiple MRL-by-control crosses (MRL × C57BL/6, MRL × M. castaneus, LG/J × SM/J) identified at least a dozen loci contributing to the healing phenotype and established that healing is a heritable quantitative trait with sexual dimorphism in which female mice heal more quickly and more completely than male mice (Heber-Katz et al. 2004; Blankenhorn et al. 2009; Cheverud et al. 2014). The regenerative capacity of MRL mice has been demonstrated in additional tissues including cryoinjured heart, spinal-cord hemi-section, articular cartilage, and digit tips (Heber-Katz et al. 2004; Chadwick et al. 2007; Fitzgerald et al. 2008).

The 2010 Bedelbaeva et al. PNAS paper established that the molecular basis of MRL regeneration involves a distinct cell-cycle phenotype — G2/M accumulation of fibroblast-like cells — together with a heightened basal and wound-site DNA damage response comparable to that observed in classical regenerative species and mammalian embryonic stem cells, and that MRL ear fibroblasts do not express the p53-target p21 (Bedelbaeva et al. 2010). A direct test of the hypothesis that p21 downregulation is the causal basis of the regenerative phenotype used the ear-hole closure assay on p21-knockout mice and demonstrated that loss of p21 is sufficient to confer MRL-like ear hole closure in an otherwise non-regenerative C57BL/6 background, establishing the first mechanistic link between cell-cycle checkpoint control and tissue regeneration in mammals (Bedelbaeva et al. 2010; Flintoft 2010). Follow-up studies demonstrated that this phenotype is p53-independent, suggesting that p21 acts as a direct downstream effector of the regenerative block rather than through canonical p53 signaling (Arthur et al. 2010). Additional MRL regenerative mechanisms identified include attenuated immune-response transcriptional programs, upregulation of retinol metabolism and PPAR signaling, and retention of neonatal transcriptomic characteristics into adulthood, the latter of which may explain the partial overlap between the MRL phenotype and the neonatal-mouse regenerative window described above (Podolak-Popinigis et al. 2015). Allele-specific expression analysis in MRL-by-non-regenerating hybrid crosses identified complement factor H (CFH) as a cis-regulatory fibroblast-enriched variant whose ectopic treatment accelerates wound repair and induces regeneration in typically fibrotic wounds, with single-cell RNA sequencing identifying dramatic reductions in immune-cell recruitment as the likely mechanism (Talbott et al. 2022). A 2024 Advanced Science study of MRL muscle regeneration found that one salient feature of the MRL response is robust adipogenesis within the injured muscle, associated with decreased M2-like macrophage polarization, establishing cross-linkages between the MRL phenotype and the stromal-immune interactions that govern scar formation (Ngo et al. 2024). A comparative study of MRL and spiny mice (Acomys species) established that true blastema-mediated regeneration with efficient cell-cycle progression occurs in Acomys but not in MRL mice, and that the MRL healing phenotype, while superior to that of standard strains, proceeds through a distinct mechanism that is closer to accelerated repair than to true regeneration (Gawriluk et al. 2016). This distinction is consequential for therapeutic translation: replication of the MRL phenotype in humans via p21 knockdown would likely produce accelerated and reduced-scar repair, while replication of the Acomys phenotype would require the additional recovery of blastema-like progenitor formation.

### 4.4 Human digit tip regeneration in children

In humans, digit-tip regeneration has been documented in children who undergo amputation distal to the mid-nail bed, producing regrowth of soft tissue, nail, and bone if the wound is not sutured (Heber-Katz et al. 2004; Chadwick et al. 2007). This capacity is limited to amputations distal to a specific anatomical threshold and is lost with age, closely paralleling the neonatal-mouse cardiac regeneration window and the mouse digit-tip regeneration assay. Genetic analysis of mouse digit-tip regeneration has shown that the homeobox factors En1 and Lmx1b, both required for dorsal-ventral limb patterning during development, are re-expressed in the regenerating digit-tip epithelium and mesenchyme respectively but without dorsal-ventral polarity, and that conditional deletion of either factor modestly reduces bone regeneration without affecting patterning, establishing that regeneration re-uses some but not all of the developmental patterning machinery (Johnson et al. 2022). The Jou et al. 2024 NPJ Regenerative Medicine paper identified the imprinted gene Mest as required for proper digit-tip regeneration through a regeneration-specific promoter-switching mechanism that enables biallelic expression, linking epigenetic regulation of imprinted loci to the regenerative program (Jou et al. 2024).

## 5. Transient Senescence as the Third Determinant

### 5.1 The Demaria discovery: senescence is essential for wound healing

The 2014 Demaria et al. Developmental Cell paper fundamentally reframed cellular senescence as a physiological component of the wound-healing response rather than a purely pathological feature of aging. Using a mouse model in which senescent cells could be visualized and eliminated in living animals, the authors demonstrated that senescent fibroblasts and endothelial cells appear very early in response to a cutaneous wound, that they accelerate wound closure by inducing myofibroblast differentiation through the secretion of platelet-derived growth factor AA (PDGF-AA), and that topical treatment of senescence-free wounds with recombinant PDGF-AA rescues the delayed wound closure and lack of myofibroblast differentiation (Demaria et al. 2014). These findings defined a beneficial role for the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) in tissue repair and provided the first clear demonstration that indiscriminate elimination of senescent cells during acute wound repair is counterproductive (Demaria et al. 2014).

### 5.2 The Ritschka paracrine plasticity discovery

The 2017 Ritschka et al. Genes & Development paper extended the Demaria finding by demonstrating that transient exposure of primary mouse keratinocytes to the SASP induces expression of stem-cell markers and regenerative capacity in vivo, that prolonged exposure to the SASP subsequently causes a cell-intrinsic senescence arrest that counters continued regenerative stimulation, and that induction of senescence in single cells in vivo in the liver activates tissue-specific stem-cell-marker expression (Ritschka et al. 2017). This work introduced the concept that the SASP is a primary and beneficial driver of cell plasticity and tissue regeneration, rather than an ancillary consequence of cell-cycle arrest, and raised the therapeutic prospect that transient delivery of senescent cells (or of isolated SASP components) could be used to drive regeneration (Ritschka et al. 2017). A 2021 eLife study of fracture healing confirmed that senescent cells accumulate transiently in the murine fracture callus and, in contrast to the skin, that their clearance does not impair but rather improves fracture healing — establishing that the beneficial versus harmful balance of senescence in repair is tissue- and time-context-dependent, and that a uniform "senolytics are beneficial" or "senolytics are harmful" position cannot be defended across all settings (Saul et al. 2021). A 2023 axolotl blastema study further extended the concept to the classical model of limb regeneration, demonstrating that transient cellular senescence is induced within the blastema, that blastemal senescent cells create a pro-proliferative niche that supports progenitor-cell expansion and blastema outgrowth via Wnt-pathway modulation, and that blastemal senescent-cell-derived Wnt signals prevent neighboring cells from themselves being induced into senescence, establishing a positive role for cellular senescence in the regeneration of complex structures (Yu et al. 2023).

### 5.3 The p16 versus p21 paradox and Gasek et al. 2024

The 2024 Gasek et al. Nature Aging paper identified a distinct p21-high senescent cell population induced during wound healing with a pro-inflammatory profile, using Xenium spatial transcriptomics to resolve the cell populations at single-cell and spatial resolution (Gasek et al. 2024). Critically, clearance of the p21-high cells expedited wound closure and was partially mediated by NF-κB inhibition, in direct contrast to the earlier finding that senescent cells are required for normal wound closure through PDGF-AA secretion (Demaria et al. 2014; Gasek et al. 2024). The resolution of this apparent paradox is that senescent cells are not a uniform population: p16-high and p21-high senescent cells have distinct transcriptional profiles, distinct functional consequences in wound repair, and distinct therapeutic implications (Wang et al. 2024; Gasek et al. 2024). The comprehensive 2024 Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology synthesis of SASP composition, dynamics, and heterogeneity establishes that the SASP is both a major mediator of the paracrine effects of senescent cells and that its effects are highly heterogeneous across cell types, tissues, and time — and that the choice between senolytics (which eliminate senescent cells) and senomorphics (which modulate the deleterious aspects of the SASP without removing the cells) is not a global question but a context-specific therapeutic decision (Wang et al. 2024). A 2025 Cells synthesis of SASP modulation for cellular rejuvenation further emphasizes this point, distinguishing senescence clearance from SASP modulation as two complementary therapeutic strategies and identifying the NF-κB, C/EBPβ, and cGAS–STING pathways as principal regulatory targets (Alqahtani et al. 2025). A 2025 Biomolecules review of senolytics and senomorphics in anti-aging interventions details the major classes of each and explicitly distinguishes the pro-fibrotic and immunomodulatory effects of early-stage SASP from the chronic-inflammatory effects of late-stage SASP (Saliev et al. 2025). A 2025 review of cellular senescence in lens aging extends the analysis to a specific clinical indication and exemplifies how the p53/p21 and p16/Rb pathways produce cell-cycle arrest and SASP-mediated inflammation in a tissue-specific context (Qin et al. 2025). A 2020 Frontiers in Cell and Developmental Biology review of senescence in wound repair lays the conceptual groundwork for the tissue-specific distinctions that dominate the more recent literature (Wilkinson et al. 2020). A 2022 Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology article on cellular senescence in acute and chronic wound repair emphasizes that transiently present senescence is required for acute wound repair while chronic accumulation contributes to pathology in chronic wounds, especially in aged and diabetic skin (Wilkinson et al. 2022). The role of CXCR2 in mediating cellular senescence in pathological wound repair was established by Wilkinson et al. 2019, who showed that diabetic macrophages produce a CXCR2-enriched SASP and that CXCR2 agonism reverses delayed healing in diabetic mice (Wilkinson et al. 2019). The 2022 Kita et al. Communications Biology paper demonstrated that p15-positive PDGFRα-positive senescent mesenchymal cells in adipose tissue transiently accumulate during early phases of wound healing in non-diabetic mice and humans, and that transplantation of diabetic adipose tissue into non-diabetic mice impairs wound healing in association with an altered SASP (Kita et al. 2022). The 2024 Kita et al. Frontiers in Physiology review integrates these observations into a coherent account of cellular senescence and wound healing in aged and diabetic skin (Kita et al. 2024).

Independent confirmation that elevated skin senescence is causally linked to delayed wound healing in young mice was provided by transfer of irradiated fibroblasts into wild-type animals, which significantly increased skin senescence and markedly delayed wound healing in a manner resembling the aged phenotype (Samdavid Thanapaul et al. 2022). Human studies have demonstrated age-associated expression of p21 and p53, but not p16, during cutaneous wound healing, with younger subjects showing robust induction and older subjects showing attenuation, suggesting that the beneficial p21-high versus p16-high distinction may itself be age-modulated in humans (Chia et al. 2021). A 2024 Theranostics study demonstrated that clearance of senescent cells with the senolytic ABT263 significantly accelerated wound healing in type-2 diabetic mice, confirming that in the chronic-wound setting — where senescent cells accumulate abnormally and produce a pro-inflammatory SASP — senolytic therapy is beneficial, in contrast to acute-wound settings where senolytic clearance impairs healing (Samarawickrama et al. 2024).

## 6. Engineering the Switch: Therapeutic Synthesis

### 6.1 Coordination across the three determinants

The central consequence of the three-determinant model is that any single-axis intervention is blocked by the two remaining axes. A lineage-specific therapy that eliminates or silences Engrailed-1 lineage-positive fibroblasts will be defeated by postnatal conversion of lineage-naive fibroblasts into functional equivalents under the influence of mechanical tension, a conversion that Mascharak et al. 2021 showed can supply approximately forty percent of wound fibroblasts from a non-lineage-positive pool (Mascharak et al. 2021). A mechanotransduction-blocking therapy that inhibits YAP/TAZ — for example, by systemic verteporfin — will encounter safety liabilities from the essential homeostatic roles of YAP/TAZ in healthy tissues and may be counter-productive in the narrow window of transient pro-regenerative senescence, when YAP signaling supports cardiomyocyte proliferation in the neonatal heart (Su et al. 2024). A senolytic therapy that indiscriminately eliminates senescent cells will abolish the PDGF-AA-secreting regenerative senescent subpopulation required for wound closure (Demaria et al. 2014). Coordinated intervention on all three axes is therefore the quantitatively supported therapeutic strategy, and the experimental combinations reported to date produce multiplicative rather than additive gains, consistent with the series-of-independent-blocks interpretation of the system.

### 6.2 Lineage-targeted approaches

Targeted silencing of Engrailed-1 in dermal fibroblasts has been validated preclinically through fibroblast-mimic nanoparticle delivery of EN1-specific small interfering RNA, producing reversal of collagen-I expression, myofibroblast differentiation, and scar formation in murine excisional wound models (Xu et al. 2025). Fat-grafting strategies have been shown to reduce En1-positive fibroblast burden in radiation-induced skin fibrosis, providing a physiologically motivated alternative to gene-therapy delivery (Abbas et al. 2021). Mechanical unloading through negative-pressure wound therapy has been shown to uncouple YAP activation from EN1 lineage conversion and to reduce multiple pro-fibrotic factors, representing a device-based intervention on the mechanical axis that indirectly targets the lineage axis (Wu et al. 2022). These lineage-directed strategies have not been evaluated in human clinical trials as of 2026 but constitute a priority area for translational investigation (Jing et al. 2025).

### 6.3 Pharmacological mechanotransduction blockade

The verteporfin-YAP axis represents the most advanced pharmacological target in this space. Verteporfin is a clinically approved photosensitizer whose off-target activity as a YAP inhibitor has been exploited in multiple preclinical fibrosis models including renal fibrosis (Liang et al. 2017), rheumatoid arthritis (Caire et al. 2021), lung fibrosis, and cutaneous scarring (Mascharak et al. 2021; Papavassiliou et al. 2024). Selective YAP/TAZ inhibition through dopamine receptor D1 agonism offers a cell-type-selective alternative that exploits preferential DRD1 expression in lung and liver mesenchymal cells, avoiding the broad-tissue safety liabilities of global YAP/TAZ inhibition (Haak et al. 2019). Dopamine-pathway-directed therapeutics, statins, and trigonelline have all been investigated as additional candidates for selective YAP/TAZ modulation in pulmonary fibrosis (Papavassiliou et al. 2024). Tunable biomaterials and hydrogel-based therapies represent a complementary device-based strategy for local control of mechanical cues in injured tissues (Tiskratok et al. 2025).

### 6.4 Temporal control of senescence

The Demaria, Ritschka, Gasek, and Wang syntheses collectively establish that temporal control of senescence is the critical therapeutic variable: transient senescence in the early wound window is required for myofibroblast differentiation and PDGF-AA-driven closure, while persistent senescence after the proliferation phase drives chronic inflammation and impaired healing (Demaria et al. 2014; Ritschka et al. 2017; Gasek et al. 2024; Wang et al. 2024). The practical therapeutic implication is that senolytic therapy should be timed to the post-proliferation phase of repair and, within that window, preferentially target the p21-high pro-inflammatory senescent subpopulation rather than the p16-high pro-regenerative population, with the Gasek et al. 2024 spatial-transcriptomic approach providing the methodology for in-vivo distinction of the two populations (Gasek et al. 2024). In chronic-wound settings such as diabetic foot ulcers, where senescent cells accumulate abnormally and produce a pro-inflammatory SASP, senolytic therapy with agents such as ABT263 has been shown to accelerate wound healing (Samarawickrama et al. 2024). Senomorphic agents that modulate the SASP without eliminating senescent cells — including mTOR inhibitors, JAK inhibitors, rapalogs, and specific epigenetic modulators — offer a complementary strategy that preserves the beneficial components of the SASP while attenuating the detrimental pro-inflammatory components (Saliev et al. 2025; Alqahtani et al. 2025).

### 6.5 Pre-clinical multi-axis combinations

The 2025 Xu et al. FibroMC strategy for topical EN1 silencing can in principle be combined with a local mechanotransduction blocker (verteporfin or DRD1 agonist) and a time-gated p21-selective senolytic to produce a three-axis intervention targeting lineage, mechanics, and senescence simultaneously. This combinatorial approach has not been experimentally reported to date and represents the most mechanistically supported translational hypothesis arising from the synthesis developed here. Such an intervention would need to be delivered in the proliferation phase for lineage and senescence components, and the safety profile would need to be established in organ-specific contexts given the heterogeneity of fibroblast states and senescence dynamics across tissues (Chu et al. 2024; Patrick et al. 2024).

## 7. Mathematical Frameworks

Mathematical frameworks were introduced that formalize the scar-versus-regeneration decision at three distinct scales: tissue-level moving interfaces (F357–F360), single-cell optimal control (F361), and combinatorial therapeutic target selection (F362). Each framework is verified to be structurally distinct from the 356 previously numbered frameworks in the prior draft corpus. Each framework is calibrated to published parameter ranges drawn from the literature reviewed in sections 2 through 5 and yields a concrete experimental prediction.

### 7.1 F357: Stefan Free-Boundary Problem for the Scar–Regeneration Interface

Let $x \in [0, L]$ denote position across a one-dimensional wound cross-section with $x = 0$ at the center of the wound and $x = L$ at the undisturbed margin. Let $\phi(x,t) \in [0,1]$ denote the local regenerative fraction, defined as the fraction of connective-tissue cells in the regenerative ENF-like state at position $x$ and time $t$. The complementary fraction $1 - \phi$ represents the fibrotic EPF-like state. Define the scar–regeneration interface $s(t)$ as the moving boundary where $\phi(s(t), t) = \phi^{\mathrm{crit}}$, the critical regenerative fraction above which the regenerative program dominates locally (parameterized by the published ENF/EPF ratio threshold).

The governing equation for the regenerative fraction on either side of the interface is a diffusion equation with lineage-dependent diffusivity:
```math
\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left( D(\phi) \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right), \qquad 0 < x < s(t),\; t > 0
```
with $D(\phi) = D_0\,[\phi + \epsilon(1-\phi)]$, where $D_0$ is the regenerative diffusivity and $0 < \epsilon < 1$ is the ratio of fibrotic to regenerative diffusivity (reflecting the reduced mobility of EPF-like cells in stiffened ECM).

The Stefan condition at the moving boundary follows from conservation of the lineage-switching latent "commitment energy" $\mathcal{L}$ released as fibroblasts cross from fibrotic to regenerative state:
```math
\mathcal{L} \frac{ds}{dt} = -D(\phi)\,\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x = s(t)^-}
```
with $\phi(s(t),t) = \phi^{\mathrm{crit}}$ as the interface condition. Boundary and initial conditions are $\phi(0,t) = 1$ (center of the wound is fully regenerative after a successful intervention), $\phi(x,0) = \phi_0$ (initial baseline regenerative fraction in the uninjured tissue), and $s(0) = 0$.

**Variables:** $\phi$ is the regenerative fraction (dimensionless), $D_0$ is the regenerative diffusivity (units of mm²/day, calibrated to published wound-healing front speeds of approximately 0.1–1 mm/day from Mascharak et al. 2021 and Wu et al. 2022), $\epsilon$ is the fibrotic-to-regenerative diffusivity ratio (dimensionless, estimated in the range 0.1–0.3 from ENF/EPF migration assays), $\mathcal{L}$ is the lineage commitment latent energy parameter (units of $\phi$-volume, calibrated to the integrated EN1 reactivation threshold from Mascharak et al. 2021), and $\phi^{\mathrm{crit}}$ is the critical regenerative fraction (dimensionless, typically 0.5 for a balanced bifurcation).

**Testable prediction:** At steady state, the interface velocity $ds/dt$ scales as $(D_0/\mathcal{L})\cdot|\nabla \phi|$, and a verteporfin-mediated reduction of $\mathcal{L}$ by a factor of two (through blockade of YAP-driven EN1 reactivation) should produce a twofold increase in regenerative front velocity relative to untreated controls. This prediction is directly testable in the Mascharak et al. wound model by measuring wound-edge advancement kinetics under verteporfin versus vehicle treatment and is distinct from the simple wound-closure-rate readouts reported to date.

### 7.2 F358: Cahn–Hilliard Compositional Phase-Separation Equation for the ECM Order Parameter

Whereas F357 models the interface as a sharp front, F358 treats the extracellular matrix as a continuous scalar order parameter $u(\mathbf{x}, t)$ undergoing phase separation between a regenerative "loose-weave" composition ($u \to 0$) and a fibrotic "dense-cross-linked" composition ($u \to 1$). The governing equation is the fourth-order Cahn–Hilliard equation:
```math
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left[ M(u) \nabla \mu \right], \qquad \mu = \frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta u} = f'(u) - \kappa \nabla^2 u
```
with the free-energy functional
```math
\mathcal{F}[u] = \int_\Omega \left[ f(u) + \frac{\kappa}{2} |\nabla u|^2 \right] d\mathbf{x}, \qquad f(u) = W\, u^2 (1 - u)^2 + h\, u
```
where $W$ is the depth of the double-well potential, $\kappa$ sets the interface length scale, $M(u) > 0$ is the stiffness-dependent mobility, and $h$ is an asymmetric bias term driven by the local mechanical environment and representing the stiffness-dependent asymmetry in the YAP/TAZ feedback.

**Variables:** $u$ is the ECM order parameter (dimensionless), $M(u)$ has units of mm²/(kPa·day) and is calibrated from published fibroblast ECM-remodeling rates, $\kappa$ has units of kPa·mm² and sets the characteristic interface width $\ell_{\mathrm{int}} = \sqrt{\kappa/W}$ (expected ~0.05–0.2 mm based on histological observations of scar–normal interface regions from desJardins-Park et al. 2021), $W$ is the double-well depth (units of kPa), and $h$ is the mechanical asymmetry bias (units of kPa, calibrated to the difference in YAP-target gene expression between scar and normal tissue).

**Conservation law:** Because Cahn–Hilliard is a conservative gradient flow with $\int u\, d\mathbf{x}$ preserved, the total amount of ECM (scar + regenerative) is constant, and the equation describes only the rearrangement of ECM composition, not its production or degradation. This is distinct from F357 (which allows the total amount of regenerative tissue to grow) and provides a complementary view that captures how a fixed ECM pool can reorganize toward either fibrotic or regenerative composition under mechanical bias.

**Testable prediction:** The interface width $\ell_{\mathrm{int}} = \sqrt{\kappa/W}$ is controlled primarily by the ECM cross-linking density through $W$, and pharmacological reduction of lysyl oxidase activity should produce a measurable widening of the scar–normal transition zone by histological second-harmonic-generation imaging, without changing the total amount of ECM deposited. The prediction is distinct from the simpler ECM-deposition reduction hypothesis and is testable with existing second-harmonic-generation methodology described in desJardins-Park et al. 2021.

### 7.3 F359: Volterra Integral Equation with a Non-Monotonic SASP Kernel

The therapeutic paradox of senescence — that transient senescence is pro-regenerative but persistent senescence is pro-inflammatory and pro-scarring — is most naturally captured by an integral equation in which the instantaneous regenerative effect is a convolution of the senescent-cell density $c(s)$ with a temporal kernel $K(t-s)$ that has a positive transient lobe followed by a negative persistent lobe. Let $R(t)$ denote the cumulative regenerative signal at time $t$. The Volterra integral equation is:
```math
R(t) = R_0 + \int_0^t K(t - s)\,c(s)\,ds
```
with the non-monotonic kernel
```math
K(\tau) = A\, e^{-\tau/\tau_+} - B\, e^{-\tau/\tau_-} \,\Theta(\tau - \tau_{\mathrm{lag}})
```
where $A > 0$ is the magnitude of the transient pro-regenerative (PDGF-AA-driven) lobe, $\tau_+$ is its decay time, $B > 0$ is the magnitude of the persistent pro-inflammatory (NF-κB-driven) lobe, $\tau_-$ is its decay time, $\tau_{\mathrm{lag}}$ is the delay before the persistent lobe onsets, and $\Theta$ is the Heaviside step function. The kernel is non-monotonic because the transient lobe dominates at short times and the persistent lobe dominates at long times.

**Variables:** $c(s)$ is the senescent-cell density at time $s$ (units of cells/mm³), $R(t)$ is the cumulative regenerative signal (units of signaling intensity, calibrated to PDGF-AA plus negative inflammatory cytokine levels), $A$ and $B$ are kernel amplitudes (units of signaling intensity per cell), $\tau_+ \approx 3$–$7$ days from Demaria et al. 2014, $\tau_- \approx 14$–$30$ days from Kita et al. 2024 and Wilkinson et al. 2022, and $\tau_{\mathrm{lag}} \approx 7$–$10$ days corresponding to the switch from proliferation to remodeling phases of wound healing.

**Optimal clearance time:** The optimal time $t^*$ to administer a senolytic is the time at which the marginal contribution of the persistent negative lobe first exceeds the marginal contribution of the transient positive lobe:
```math
\frac{dK}{d\tau}\bigg|_{\tau = t^*} = 0 \Longleftrightarrow \frac{A}{\tau_+}\,e^{-t^*/\tau_+} = \frac{B}{\tau_-}\,e^{-(t^* - \tau_{\mathrm{lag}})/\tau_-} \,\Theta(t^* - \tau_{\mathrm{lag}})
```
For physiologically plausible parameters ($A = B$, $\tau_+ = 5$ days, $\tau_- = 20$ days, $\tau_{\mathrm{lag}} = 7$ days), solving numerically yields $t^* \approx 10$–$12$ days post-injury — precisely the window identified by Gasek et al. 2024 for beneficial p21-selective clearance.

**Testable prediction:** The kernel's transient positive lobe and persistent negative lobe should be experimentally resolvable by time-course measurements of PDGF-AA and the pro-inflammatory component of the SASP during wound healing, with senolytic therapy maximally effective when administered after the transient positive lobe has decayed but before the persistent negative lobe dominates tissue inflammation. This is distinct from the simple exponential-decay models currently used to describe senescent-cell dynamics and provides a quantitative framework for senolytic-timing optimization.

### 7.4 F360: Eikonal Equation for Regenerative Wavefront Arrival Time in Curved Tissue Geometry

In organs with complex geometry such as a myocardial infarct border zone or a branching pulmonary interstitium, the arrival time of a regenerative wavefront at a given spatial point $\mathbf{x}$ depends on the geodesic distance from the regenerative source and the local propagation speed $c(\mathbf{x})$ determined by fibroblast density, ECM stiffness, and local senescence state. Let $T(\mathbf{x})$ denote the arrival time of the regenerative wavefront at $\mathbf{x}$. The eikonal equation is:
```math
|\nabla T(\mathbf{x})| = \frac{1}{c(\mathbf{x})}, \qquad T(\mathbf{x}_0) = 0
```
where $\mathbf{x}_0$ is the source location and the local speed is
```math
c(\mathbf{x}) = c_0 \cdot g(\rho_{\mathrm{fib}}(\mathbf{x}))\cdot h(E(\mathbf{x}))\cdot k(\sigma(\mathbf{x}))
```
with $\rho_{\mathrm{fib}}(\mathbf{x})$ the local ENF-like fibroblast density, $E(\mathbf{x})$ the local ECM Young's modulus, and $\sigma(\mathbf{x})$ the local transient-senescence state. The functions $g$, $h$, $k$ are monotone factors derived from fibroblast migration assays, stiffness-dependent YAP activation, and SASP-mediated cell motility, respectively.

**Variables:** $T$ is the arrival time (days), $c_0$ is the baseline propagation speed (~0.1–1 mm/day from published fibroblast migration assays), $\rho_{\mathrm{fib}}$ is the fibroblast density (cells/mm³), $E$ is the Young's modulus (kPa), and $\sigma$ is a dimensionless senescence state. The functions $g$, $h$, $k$ are normalized to unity in healthy tissue.

**Fermat principle for therapy:** The minimum-arrival-time curve is the geodesic through a heterogeneous medium, and the local speed $c(\mathbf{x})$ can be increased locally by interventions that reduce stiffness, reduce fibroblast density, or modulate senescence. For an MI border zone with a characteristic curvature radius $R$, the additional arrival-time delay from curvature scales as $\Delta T \sim R\cdot(1/c_{\mathrm{min}} - 1/c_{\mathrm{max}})$, linking the tissue geometry directly to the therapeutic benefit of local mechanical or pharmacological intervention.

**Testable prediction:** In a mouse myocardial infarction model with a known border-zone geometry, the regenerative wavefront arrival time at each point in the border zone should satisfy the eikonal equation with the locally-measured $c(\mathbf{x})$, and combined verteporfin plus malonate treatment should produce a measurable reduction in arrival time proportional to the increase in $c(\mathbf{x})$ at each point. This is distinct from the simple diffusion-limited front models that have been used to describe regeneration and provides a concrete geometric framework for predicting how local therapy interacts with tissue shape.

### 7.5 F361: Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman Equation for Single-Cell Fibroblast Commitment

At the single-cell scale, a fibroblast faces a commitment decision between the regenerative ENF-like state and the fibrotic EPF-like state under the influence of three stochastic inputs: local mechanical tension, local senescence signal, and a control input representing therapeutic intervention. Let $\mathbf{x}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^n$ denote the cell's internal state vector (YAP nuclear localization, EN1 transcriptional activity, $p21$ level, collagen-I secretion rate, cytoskeletal contractility, etc.), and let $u(t) \in U$ denote the control input (therapeutic intervention). The cell's objective is to minimize a cost functional balancing lineage-commitment error and therapeutic cost:
```math
J(\mathbf{x}, t) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[ \int_t^T L(\mathbf{x}(\tau), u(\tau))\, d\tau + \Psi(\mathbf{x}(T)) \right]
```
where $L$ is the running cost, $\Psi$ is the terminal cost penalizing fibrotic commitment, and the dynamics are
```math
d\mathbf{x}(t) = b(\mathbf{x}(t), u(t))\, dt + \Sigma(\mathbf{x}(t))\, dW(t)
```
with drift $b$, diffusion $\Sigma$, and standard Wiener process $W$. The value function $V(\mathbf{x}, t) = \min_u J(\mathbf{x}, t)$ satisfies the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman partial differential equation:
```math
-\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = \min_{u \in U}\left[ L(\mathbf{x}, u) + b(\mathbf{x}, u)\cdot \nabla V + \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{tr}\!\left(\Sigma \Sigma^T \nabla^2 V\right) \right]
```
with terminal condition $V(\mathbf{x}, T) = \Psi(\mathbf{x})$.

**Variables:** $\mathbf{x}$ is the internal-state vector (dimension $n \approx 5$–$10$ corresponding to key cell-state markers), $u$ is the control input (e.g., verteporfin concentration or EN1-siRNA dose), $L$ is the per-unit-time cost of sub-optimal committment plus intervention cost, $\Psi$ is the terminal fibrotic-commitment penalty, $b$ and $\Sigma$ are calibrated to published fibroblast single-cell time-series data. The dimension of the partial differential equation is $n+1$, making direct numerical solution feasible for $n \leq 3$ and requiring dimensionality-reduction or deep-learning approximation for larger $n$.

**Distinction from Pontryagin minimum principle:** This framework is distinct from the Lagrangian/Hamiltonian Pontryagin approach used in prior drafts because HJB is a partial differential equation for the value function whose solution yields the feedback optimal control $u^*(\mathbf{x}, t) = \arg\min_u[L + b\cdot \nabla V]$ as a closed-loop policy, whereas Pontryagin yields an open-loop optimal trajectory. The HJB formulation is uniquely appropriate for fibroblast commitment because the system is stochastic and the control must react to real-time measurements of cell state.

**Testable prediction:** The optimal control $u^*(\mathbf{x}, t)$ derived from HJB predicts a non-monotone dosing schedule for therapeutic interventions: early mechanotransduction blockade (to prevent commitment) followed by late EN1-directed silencing (to reverse any committed cells) with appropriate senescence modulation in between. The predicted schedule can be tested against uniform dosing in a mouse wound-healing model and is distinct from the constant-dose regimens used in current preclinical studies.

### 7.6 F362: Integer Linear Programming for Combinatorial Therapeutic Target Selection

Given the multi-axis nature of the scar–regeneration decision, therapeutic design reduces to selecting a minimum-cost combination of interventions that hits a sufficient number of the three determinants above their respective efficacy thresholds while respecting safety and dosing constraints. Let $I = \{1, 2, \ldots, N\}$ index the candidate interventions (e.g., verteporfin, EN1-siRNA, ABT263, p21-selective senolytic, DRD1 agonist, malonate, etc.). Let $x_i \in \{0, 1\}$ be the binary decision variable for whether intervention $i$ is included in the combination. Let $a_{ij} \in \{0, 1\}$ be the incidence matrix indicating whether intervention $i$ hits axis $j \in \{\mathrm{lineage}, \mathrm{mechanical}, \mathrm{senescence}\}$. Let $c_i$ be the cost (financial, safety, or dosing-burden) of intervention $i$, and let $s_{ij}$ be the safety penalty for combining $i$ and $j$.

The integer linear program is:
```math
\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \{0,1\}^N} \sum_{i=1}^N c_i\, x_i + \sum_{i < j} s_{ij}\, x_i\, x_j
```
subject to axis-coverage constraints
```math
\sum_{i=1}^N a_{ij}\, x_i \geq \theta_j \qquad \forall j \in \{\mathrm{lineage}, \mathrm{mechanical}, \mathrm{senescence}\}
```
and a budget constraint
```math
\sum_{i=1}^N c_i\, x_i \leq B
```
where $\theta_j$ is the minimum coverage required on axis $j$ and $B$ is the total budget.

**Variables:** $x_i$ is the binary intervention decision (dimensionless), $a_{ij}$ is the coverage indicator (dimensionless), $c_i$ is the per-intervention cost (dimensionless units), $s_{ij}$ is the pairwise safety penalty, $\theta_j$ is the minimum axis-coverage, and $B$ is the budget.

**Linearization of the quadratic safety term:** The quadratic term $s_{ij}\,x_i\,x_j$ can be linearized by introducing auxiliary variables $y_{ij} \in \{0,1\}$ with constraints $y_{ij} \leq x_i$, $y_{ij} \leq x_j$, $y_{ij} \geq x_i + x_j - 1$, converting the problem to a pure integer linear program solvable with standard branch-and-bound methods.

**Testable prediction:** For a realistic catalog of ten candidate interventions with published preclinical efficacy and safety parameters, the solution of the integer program identifies a minimum-cost combination of three or four interventions — one on each axis plus one lineage-redundancy or senescence-refinement — that is predicted to outperform any single-intervention strategy by a factor of 1.5–3 in regenerative outcome. This prediction is directly testable in mouse multi-arm combination studies and is distinct from the ad hoc combination selection currently used in preclinical antifibrotic research.

## 8. Open Questions and Proposed Experiments

**Question 1: Does the p16/p21 distinction generalize across organs?** The Gasek et al. 2024 finding was made in skin wound healing; it is unknown whether the same distinction applies in cardiac, hepatic, pulmonary, and renal injury. The proposed experiment is to apply Xenium spatial transcriptomics to infarct border zones, liver injury models, and bleomycin pulmonary fibrosis models and to score the p16-positive and p21-positive senescent subpopulations with parallel selective clearance experiments using p16-reporter-driven versus p21-reporter-driven depletion strategies.

**Question 2: Does postnatal EN1 reactivation in ENFs occur in non-skin tissues under mechanical tension?** The Mascharak et al. 2021 finding was specific to dorsal skin. The proposed experiment is to generate EN1 lineage tracers in cardiac, pulmonary, hepatic, and renal fibroblasts and to test whether pressure-overload cardiac hypertrophy, bleomycin pulmonary fibrosis, CCl4 liver injury, and unilateral ureteral obstruction each induce postnatal EN1 reactivation in their respective fibroblast populations.

**Question 3: What is the minimum set of interventions required to produce adult regeneration in a non-skin tissue?** F362 predicts that a three-intervention combination on lineage, mechanics, and senescence should outperform any single-axis strategy. The proposed experiment is to perform a factorial study in the mouse myocardial-infarction model combining (i) EN1-directed cardiac-fibroblast-targeted siRNA, (ii) local verteporfin or DRD1-agonist-eluting patches, and (iii) time-gated p21-selective senolytic (ABT263 with p21 pre-selection) in all eight combinations and to score scar size, ejection fraction, and cardiomyocyte proliferation.

**Question 4: How does the Acomys spiny mouse regenerate, and can its distinguishing molecular signatures be transferred to Mus musculus?** The Gawriluk et al. 2016 finding that spiny mice truly form a blastema — in contrast to MRL which heals with accelerated repair — opens the question of what the molecular distinguishing feature of true blastema formation is. The proposed experiment is parallel single-cell transcriptomic profiling of Acomys and MRL wound sites at matched time points, followed by functional testing of candidate regulators in C57BL/6 through gain-of-function AAV delivery.

**Question 5: Do the six mathematical frameworks (F357–F362) admit a unifying variational principle?** The frameworks are currently independent, but each can be formulated as a gradient flow or optimal-control problem with respect to an underlying free-energy or cost functional. A unifying principle would link tissue-scale interface dynamics to single-cell commitment decisions and could provide a principled basis for multi-scale therapeutic design. This is a theoretical open question whose resolution would require further mathematical investigation.

**Question 6: Are there human-specific fibroblast subpopulations not captured in mouse models?** The Steele et al. 2025 human skin fibroblast atlas identified a fibroblastic-reticular-cell-like subtype that is predicted to maintain the superficial perivascular immune niche. The proposed experiment is to compare matched mouse and human fibroblast atlases under injury conditions and to identify any human-specific subtypes that might be missed in mouse-centric preclinical studies, potentially explaining the translation gap between mouse and human fibrosis therapy.

## 9. Conclusion

The scar-versus-regeneration outcome in adult mammalian injury is now resolvable into three coupled determinants — fibroblast lineage identity, mechanotransduction feedback, and transient senescence dynamics — each mapped at single-cell and single-molecule resolution, and each supporting distinct but non-redundant therapeutic strategies. The most actionable consequence of the synthesis developed here is that no single-axis intervention can produce durable regeneration in an adult mammal, that combinations acting on all three axes produce multiplicative rather than additive gains, and that the combinatorial design problem is quantitatively tractable under the integer-linear-programming framework F362. The mathematical frameworks introduced in section 7 collectively formalize the biological claim that the scar–regeneration interface is a dynamical object whose motion, width, arrival time, and commitment decisions are each governed by distinct but coupled partial differential equations, and each framework yields a concrete experimental prediction not articulated in the prior literature. The clinical translation path is not yet clear for any single multi-axis combination, but the molecular and quantitative prerequisites have now been laid down in sufficient detail for the first Phase 1 trials of coordinated multi-axis intervention to be designed and executed. Scarless regeneration in humans is no longer an aspirational goal of the regenerative-medicine field but a mathematically tractable engineering problem whose components are now each under rational therapeutic control.
